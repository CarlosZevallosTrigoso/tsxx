### B3 · El argumento en sus propias frases (tres pasajes enteros)

**a) Dónde anuncia qué va a hacer** (p188-189, líneas 187-199):
> «Paradoxically, the pandemic also placed the Amazon at the centre of a global conversation about the relevance of Indigenous knowledge as an alternative to Western thinking and a tool for imagining post- capitalist futures. It is no coincidence, then, that only in the years following the pandemic, Indigenous Peruvian artists have achieved what until recently seemed distant: representation in commercial galleries, participation in international fairs and biennials, and the incorporation of their works into the collections of the world's leading museums. It is precisely in this context, traversed by deep contradictions, that we believe it is vital to review the SHRA's work and the ways in which its seminal labour has shaped what we know today as Amazonian Indigenous art in Peru, laying the foundations for its consolidation in the international contemporary cultural panorama.»

**b) Dónde enuncia su tesis** (p199-200, líneas 608-617):
> «We believe that the method followed by the SHRA –  based on the passage from the oral and the performative to writing and painting –  shaped the way in which the idea of contemporary Indigenous art was configured in Peru. At the heart of the projects carried out with the artists was the intention of learning and preserving the knowledge of their communities. It was, therefore, a method aligned more closely to anthropological or ethnographic frameworks, though it consistently acknowledged the individuality of each production. In other words, each artist shared the knowledge of their community through their unique experiences and perspectives, expressing it in highly personal styles and forms.»

**c) Dónde cierra** (p203, líneas 752-770):
> «Precisely for this reason, we believe that the work of the SHRA is a paradigmatic case in Peru. Despite being an institution rooted in the heart of academia, its interdisciplinary team always had the conviction that self- determination was essential when working with artists from Indigenous communities. To this end, the SHRA not only broke with traditional academic hierarchies, prioritising their voices and perspectives, but also underwent a process of critical deconstruction of its own disciplinary frameworks –  historical, ethnographic, anthropological, among others –  which allowed for the full recognition of the individual artistic authorship of the Indigenous artists with whom it collaborated. This shift in focus was crucial in transforming the SHRA into a flexible and malleable 'contact zone' where Indigenous artists could interact with hegemonic structures on their own terms, challenging the colonial logics of erasure that had marked the history of local art. Moreover, as we have pointed out, the seminar paved the way for a pioneering generation of Amazonian Indigenous artists to claim their central place in contemporary Peruvian art, while also becoming a platform for the resignification of the language of art in Peru and for dialogue within the global art circuit.»

### B6 · Crisis (violencia política, terrorismo, dictadura, crisis económica, colonialidad — pasajes enteros)

**a) Colonialidad / exclusión (apertura)** (p185, líneas 10-23):
> «For much of Peru's republican history, Indigenous and vernacular artists, along with their distinctive forms of aesthetic and cultural production, have been excluded from official art history. The origins of this exclusion can be traced back to the nineteenth century, when the concept of art in Peru was aligned with the paradigms of Eurocentric modernity and disseminated –  through museums and fine arts schools –  according to Western aesthetic hierarchies. Indeed, the concept of art, based on strong European narratives and institutions and transplanted to Latin America, almost unchanged, has operated as one of the most powerful and persistent devices of exclusion of Indigenous creators, whose cultural expressions were relegated to the category of crafts, generating a theoretical and ethical impasse that, at least in the case of Peru, has marked the history of art until the present.»

**b) Carácter racializado** (p185-186, líneas 24-36):
> «While it is undeniable that the dichotomy between art and crafts exists and persists in other contexts, what distinguishes the Peruvian case is its deeply racialised nature. On one side of this duality, we find European art, which claims to be universal... On the other side, objects created by Indigenous authors, especially those that do not imitate Western styles or lack academic training, are relegated to the realm of the exotic and even the primitive. It is this polarising logic that, until the end of the twentieth century in Peru, led to denial of the possibility that something could be simultaneously art and Indigenous.»

**c) Dictadura / Gobierno Revolucionario de Velasco** (p187, líneas 84-101):
> «Another important milestone took place in the 1970s, during the Gobierno Revolucionario de las Fuerzas Armadas (Revolutionary Government of the Armed Forces, GRFA) of General Juan Velasco Alvarado (1968– 1975), when crafts were politically redefined and positioned as a central component of national culture. This effort had two main features. Firstly, the Premio Nacional de Fomento a la Cultura... was awarded to Joaquín López Antay, a retablista (image maker) from Ayacucho, in 1975... Secondly, a plan was implemented to transform the political economy of folk art, integrating the artisan into a guild –  the artisanate –  with intellectual property rights over their creations... but it also implied the 'proletarianisation of a collective in relation to its own artistic production'.»

**d) Época del caucho (explotación)** (p192, líneas 336-338):
> «Churay represented the natural environment, the myths, stories, and ritual practices of his community (see Figure 8.1), as well as the exploitation suffered by the Bora people during the rubber era.»

**e) COVID-19 / abandono estatal** (p188, líneas 181-185):
> «Yet, this growing recognition in the cultural sphere revealed a structural limit in 2020, when a COVID- 19 infection rate of 70 percent made the Amazonian Indigenous communities the most vulnerable population to the virus in Peru, thus evidencing the historical neglect they have suffered at the hands of the state.»

**f) Colonialidad / segregación institucionalizada** (p200, líneas 628-633):
> «The work of the SHRA has been seminal and essential in dismantling what, for much of Peru's republican history, constituted a system of institutionalised segregation against the artistic expressions of Indigenous peoples. However, and perhaps unintentionally, this project defined the 'integration' of Amazonian Indigenous art under a logic closer to the modern than to the contemporary.»

**g) Desplazamientos forzados / caucho (Santiago Yahuarcani)** (nota 15, líneas 823-827):
> «he traces his roots to La Chorrera in the Colombian Amazon, from where his community migrated to the Ampiyacu River region in the Peruvian Amazon due to the forced displacements of Indigenous populations during the rubber boom... explores Indigenous knowledge, Amazonian ontologies and the genocidal policies inflicted upon his ancestors.»

### B8 · Brecha (algo se volvió insostenible / quiebre / ruptura — pasajes enteros)

**a)** (p186, líneas 52-64) — Lauer, momentos de ruptura:
> «Lauer affirms that this complex multipolar structure was reproduced in other countries in the region, with Indigenous majorities remaining on the margins of power. Throughout the text he identifies different moments of rupture or fracturing of their hierarchies. A first key episode occurred in the 1920s, when the artists of the Indigenist movement, trained in the European fine arts, began to observe with interest the visual production of the rest of the country, incorporating into their works elements inspired by contemporary vernacular forms and the pre- Hispanic past. Although this unprecedented interest in the Indigenous... represented a turning point in the history of Peruvian art, the Indigenist artists' tendency toward aestheticisation from a telluric perspective... often contradicted their visual practice, which failed to delve deeper into the 'aesthetic thinking of the dominated'.»

**b)** (p186-187, líneas 103-116) — el giro de los 90 como cambio de marco:
> «More recently, during the 1990s, the so- called global 'Indigenous turn' marked a crucial change. During much of the twentieth century and in the face of the various agrarian reforms that took place throughout Latin America, class was the privileged framework for understanding Indigenous identity... However, during the 1980s, the focus on social class began to lose relevance as Indigenous communities adopted new identities as First Nations, claiming historical rights over the territories they had inhabited since ancestral times. This process, facilitated in part by the work of anthropologists and non- governmental organizations (NGOs), coincided with the rise of neoliberal multiculturalism, positioning Indigeneity as a central category that allowed communities to articulate and claim rights both locally and internationally.»

**c)** (p189-190, líneas 261-267) — punto de quiebre para el SHRA:
> «Prior to working with Indigenous artists, the SHRA had already conducted research on the Amazon from historical and archaeological perspectives. However, as Soria points out, these investigations analysed the region 'from the viewpoint of the colonizing and mestizo society'. Although it is evident today that native communities are also producers of history, many scholars of the time 'were dominated by the erroneous concept that societies lacking alphabetic writing were ahistorical'. A turning point for the SHRA was the work that graphic artist Gredna Landolt developed with the teachers of the Programa de Formación de Maestros Bilingües de la Amazonía Peruana...»

### B9 · Procedencia (invención / origen / primera formulación; algo que viene de antes o de otro sitio — pasajes enteros)

**a)** El «giro indígena» es global, no local, y precede al caso peruano (p186-187, líneas 103-116; ver también B8-b). «the so- called global 'Indigenous turn'».

**b)** Origen de la categoría «arte popular» atribuido a los indigenistas (p186, líneas 69-78):
> «On the other hand, as historians Gabriela Germaná and Amy Bowman- McElhone have pointed out, the Indigenists played a key role in creating the category of arte popular (popular art) by studying and promoting the works of rural and native artists in Lima. This initiative, which sought to establish the visual arts of the 'people' as the foundation and bastion of a national art history, failed to dismantle the aesthetic hierarchies and social inequalities...»

**c)** El giro a la pintura figurativa NO se origina en el SHRA (p199, líneas 562-570):
> «The shift towards figurative painting on two- dimensional support, however, did not originate in the SHRA. ### B13 · Lo que quedó fuera (pasajes más extensos no usados antes)

**a)** Sección II, sobre el método colaborativo del SHRA (p188-189, líneas 206-215):
> «In the 1990s, the seminar began working with Indigenous artists from the Andes and the Amazon to recover the memory of their peoples using the painting- writing equation as a methodology for approaching oral societies. Unlike other research projects that took the knowledge of Indigenous and rural groups to inform their studies, the SHRA identified early on that these stories should be told by the members of the communities themselves. Thus, they established collaborative projects between artists and scholars. This way of working not only empowered the artists, but also led to the development of works that would significantly define the aesthetics of Amazonian Indigenous art in Peru.»

**b)** Programa Formabiap y la propuesta de Landolt a Fundación Telefónica (p191, líneas 303-314):
> «On the other hand, after obtaining authorisation from the Formabiap teachers in 2000, Landolt approached Fundación Telefónica with a proposal to develop a publication that would compile the work produced through the programme. However, the foundation suggested an exhibition accompanied by a catalogue. From that moment on, Landolt began to collaborate directly with each Indigenous teacher or sage, broadening and deepening the creation of a more extensive set of drawings and paintings. In contrast to Formabiap's original approach, where the images functioned primarily as ethnographic explanations of the Indigenous peoples' way of life, knowledge and practices, Landolt's approach focused on highlighting the aesthetic and subjective value of these representations.»

**c)** «Archive» y «repertoire» / traducción de la oralidad (p198, líneas 529-551):
> «In many cases, however, the archive and 'the repertoire' work together in the transmission of knowledge. Many practices in societies with or without writing require both materiality and action at the same time. Under this premise, while the SHRA projects sought to transfer changing practices to the stability of a Western- style archive, they also recognised that 'the memory of native peoples is activated by symbolic representations: facial and body paintings, designs on pottery and hunting marks, among other semiotic images' that cannot be translated exclusively through writing. Therefore, they decided to translate the oral simultaneously into written texts and images. First, they invited the narrators to write their stories in a bilingual format (in their native language and in Spanish) and then asked them to make drawings to complement the texts. 'We wanted the texts produced in the SHRA to associate painting and words as reciprocally complementary and suitable elements to express the worldviews of the Amazonian peoples.' These processes were carried out in different ways. 'Some storytellers wanted to write and illustrate their stories themselves... Others, however, preferred to tell us their stories through drawings rather than writing them down, as was the case with Roldán Pinedo and Elena Valera. There were also those who wrote and let other native painters illustrate their stories. This happened with the Shipibo Herminio Vásquez (storyteller) and Robert Rengifo (painter).'»

**d)** Figurativismo como criterio de legitimación / medios no reconocidos (p200, líneas 635-654):
> «Indeed, the seminar's ethnographic intent and working methodology, expanded through its artists and circles of influence, led Peruvian Amazonian Indigenous art to adopt, primarily, the form of figurative painting. Established as the hegemonic medium of Eurocentric artistic modernity in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, and apparently superseded by contemporary standards, painting then became the criterion of legitimisation for these previously marginalised artists. This, we believe, may have limited the experimentation of Amazonian Indigenous artists with other media such as performance, video or installation... On the other hand, long standing traditional Amazonian techniques such as ceramics, textiles or beads –  which preserve the geometric designs characteristic of their communities –  were not equally recognised as contemporary artistic expressions.»
