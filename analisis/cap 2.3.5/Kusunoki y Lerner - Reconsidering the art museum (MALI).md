### B3 · El argumento en sus propias frases (tres pasajes enteros)

**(a) Qué va a hacer / marco (p. 147, líneas 7-19):**
«As the old saying goes, ‘making a virtue out of necessity’ has been the main challenge faced by the Museo de Arte de Lima (MALI) since it first opened its doors on 10 March 1961, in the heart of the historic centre of the Peruvian capital. The institution was born out of the efforts of a patronage set up by local businessmen and intellectuals, who sought to remedy the paradoxical lack of an art museum in a country which, for almost half a century, had been trying to define itself as a modern nation precisely by highlighting its ancient artistic heritage. At that moment, it was indeed impossible to incorporate representative revisions of much of the art developed in the Peruvian territory into the new museum's collections, because entire groups of heritage pieces had already been systematically extracted to satisfy the demand of the international antiques market.»

**(b) Tesis (p. 152, líneas 230-248):**
«The incorporation of the Piraq Causa, together with the establishment of an Amazonian axis, opened the way for MALI to critically reassess the work it had carried out over the previous two decades in building its historical collections. Throughout this period, the institution took up the programmatic and constant strengthening of its collections as one of its main public functions. Its commitment was to recover a wider material universe created before the twentieth century, based on two main premises. First, the lack of acquisition policies in most of Peru's museums put numerous key historical aspects of the region's visual culture at risk of dispersion or total disappearance. Second, the need to safeguard these testimonies rested on another very evident assumption premise, especially for an art museum whose collections aspire to cover a time span of thousands of years: namely, that the category of art as a differentiated field did not exist for most of that period.»

**(c) Cierre (p. 155, líneas 382-398):**
«Today, visitors to MALI will find that the narratives of the museum seek to address two distinct qualities associated with the term art, in a differentiated way. There is the narrative that explores different modalities of visual creation from a perspective that values their aesthetic quality. At the same time, the other narrative reflects on art as a constructed category. In its aspiration to forge an autonomous space, art imposes a power dynamic that draws boundaries in a much broader material universe. It is impossible to confront the myths about art without exploring what is left outside that field. In fact, considering something as art is not just an act of naming. Categories such as ‘popular art’ or ‘Indigenous art’ cannot only be understood as spaces where non- academic forms of visual production are recognised. They are creative fields that, due to their ‘artistic’ nature, also have their own boundaries. As a result, what is needed is a broad collections policy that, by reconstructing constellations of objects and connections, can provoke the awareness needed for us to place ourselves inside a set of coordinates that are historically situated, and therefore, capable of being transformed.»

### B13 · Lo que quedó fuera (pasajes más extensos no usados antes, enteros)

**(1) p. 148, líneas 40-54 — definición amplia del arte en las colecciones fundacionales:**
«Although it was largely defined by contingencies, the vast material universe that made up the institution's founding collections confronted the spectator with a relatively broad definition of art and the artistic. The collection was initially organised according to chronological criteria, divided into major periods that spanned from pre- Columbian times to early modernity – a categorisation that led to museographic display choices as well as to the current organisation into curatorial areas. Yet the works often escaped the strict demarcations of the academic canon of the fine arts. This broad definition of artistic practice was doubtlessly supported by a local scene that, from the 1920s on, had recognised the full artistic condition of ‘their own’ pre- Hispanic or viceregal expressions that didn't correspond to the international canon of fine arts. But the eclectic grouping of all kinds of objects in an art museum was also possible because the institution was independent of any teaching centres and had no other instances of official validation of the arts at that time.»

**(2) p. 148, líneas 71-88 — «Categories in tension», carácter universal del arte y su riesgo:**
«When we create relations between the material horizons of different periods and contexts, it fosters the idea that art possesses a universal character – one that enables us to conceive of it as a continuous field, capable of incorporating human differences. As if it were a projection of the current ideal of a democratic public sphere, different visual expressions can be installed within the same environment, where one can move between them on the same footing, beyond ethnic, cultural or social differences that separate their producers or consumers. But in establishing these dialogues, art museums may risk radically homogenising the material universe of the past – especially when they relegate to the background the uses and functions that make any object something more than a mere vehicle for aesthetic contemplation.»

**(3) p. 152, líneas 250-276 — sombra del pasado sobre el presente, «dismantling the dichotomous structure»:**
«Perhaps the main potential of putting together a contemporary collection was the possibility that a fluid universe of objects from the past would cast its shadow on the immediate present, to reshape it. Within this logic, the incorporation of Piraq Causa or the creation of a contemporary Amazonian collection should not simply be understood as institutional recognition of the full artistic condition of a creative field previously considered ‘popular art’ or ‘crafts’. […] Thus, MALI's institutional gesture must be understood as part of the broader process of dismantling the dichotomous structure of the Peruvian artistic field, one historically grounded in class and ethnic divisions. Piraq Causa entered the institution not only as a work of art, but also as popular art and craft, since these latter terms refer to historically determined coordinates of creation. Separating the work from those terms to arbitrarily install it in the supposedly timeless space of the ‘truly’ artistic would be, although it may sound paradoxical, a way of validating the fictions inherited from modernism.»

**(4) p. 153-154, líneas 308-329 — representación amplia, esencialismo, tradición:**
«The work of collecting strives for the very broad representation of a population made up of very diverse groups, which are not necessarily limited to geographical boundaries or traditional historical narratives of the nation-state. However, questioning this type of rhetoric must not simply lead to erecting other identitarian constructions that, because they are based on ethnic considerations, are conceived as monolithic truths in the same way as nationalist discourses. To avoid all essentialism, it is crucial to consider a concept such as tradition – understood as the genealogical line that, in any given period, tracks a form of knowledge or a repertoire of conventions. […] Traditions set out multiple parallel lines that constantly intersect, so that the spectrum of the artistic is defined as a multiplicity of stories that are not always related to the artistic field as an autonomous modern construction.»

**(5) p. 154-155, líneas 359-380 — Imaginarios contemporáneos post-COVID:**
«A few years later, when activities in the museum resumed after the COVID- 19 pandemic, the programmes carried out in the permanent collection rooms on the second floor similarly aimed to reflect the change of focus taken by the museum in the previous decade, with a more transversal and renewed museographic approach. In this context, the Imaginarios contemporáneos (Contemporary Imaginaries) exhibitions held between 2021 and 2022 sought to present an updated overview of MALI's contemporary art collections, bringing together works by Peruvian and international artists whose proposals weave together narratives about customs, worldviews and ways of understanding the territory, or explore gender as a space for artistic expression in a way that has long been lacking in art-historical narratives or narratives about the collection.»
