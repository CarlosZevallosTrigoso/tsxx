### They sing, They dance, They fight – 2020 11va Bienal de Berlín

They sing. They dance. They fight es una videoinstalación multicanal -compuesta por un trío de películas musicales. cada una de las cuales está constituida por numerosos videoclips- presentada en un entorno que, con luces de neón y música, recrea el ambiente festivo de un club.

En un contexto en el que las mujeres son asesinadas en todo el mundo, They Sing, They Dance, They Fight, gira en torno a lo que Tejada-Herrera ha llamado "la estética de la política del empoderamiento". Así, la artista utiliza la belleza, el glamour y el absurdo como herramientas para generar narrativas que redefinen las nociones de fuerza física y social femenina, ofreciendo alternativas a los roles asignados a las mujeres en el marco de una sociedad capitalista patriarcal.

El primer vídeo de la serie alterna escenas en las que una mujer "bailarina de tijeras" -danza tradicional peruana normalmente reservada a los hombres- demuestra sus habilidades, con imágenes de un grupo de mujeres y niñas cis entrenando para luchar. En el segundo vídeo, la artista creó animaciones sobre clips que mostraban más entrenamientos. Para estos entrenamientos, más mujeres cis y trans memorizaron las técnicas de autodefensa y se documentaron 37 practicando desde sus espacios privados durante la crisis sanitaria mundial del coronavirus. Una mujer trans encarna a la palla corongo, un personaje femenino típico del folclore peruano. A continuación, una sirena anciana y una mujer trans cantan canciones muy conocidas, de diversos géneros musicales, que hacen referencia metafórica a las luchas sociales.La artista cambió los pronombres de las letras de masculino a femenino y no binario.

They sing, They dance, They fight presenta a differentes grupos de mujeres que, sin pedir disculpas, interpretan construcciones alternativas de la feminidad. Sin duda, una celebración de las chicas, las mujeres trans y las mujeres cis de todas las edades; así como de la sororidad, el amor y la fuerza.

"I think that gender itself is violence, that the norms of masculinity and femininity as we know them produce violence. If we changed the ways of education for children, maybe we would modify what we call gender violence. We always think that girls can defend themselves without been aggressive. Let's be honest: in a culture of war, to not to prepare technically and practically a group of society to be able to access aggression techniques when it is needed is discriminatory. I look for radical alternatives to the culture of war, and one of those is an equalitarian access to violence techniques. Toni Negri said: we have to give weapons to the population because the state is armed. I would say: we have to give weapons to women because men are armed".

King Kong's girl befriends King Kong. She makes a choice not to return to civilization. Instead she stays with King Kong exercising some political incorrectness, and displays of strength. She did not become the submissive object of desire because what she enjoys is friendship and freedom. Like her friendship with Queen Kong. And this King Kong's girl trains to fight. Our girls will train to fight. The project is inspired by Virginie Despentes' King Kong Theory specifically her analysis on the relationship between King Kong and the woman heroine within the film King Kong. But in this installation, there is a very different turn. This is the subject of the installation presented within the proposal.

The drawings refer to the friendship between the girl and King Kong, as well as the presence of Queen Kong with her exuberant uninhibited and unapologetic sexuality. The colors in the installation emphasize the sensual character of the drawings, creating at the same time a girlish aesthetic made with bright pastel candy colors. Fake fur on objects is also present. These colors and textures create an inviting friendly atmosphere. The space is inviting and filled with humor, expressed through the drawings and the smiley gorilla masks.

The video installation follows and introduces a girl and her adventures in the city of Lima. She is our hero, and she is learning to fight. Sometimes we see her within interiors, which the camera enters presenting intimate and quotidian situations. Other times a drone allows us to see her transiting the big city. In the video installation a trainer also couches a group of girls from a local team.

The drawings within the installation are a response to an analysis presented by Virginie Despentes in her book

King Kong Theory, in the chapter King Kong Girl. Within the analysis that Virginie Despentes presents of the relationship between the giant gorilla and the woman in the film King Kong, who she calls King Kong Girl, the woman leaves the gorilla and returns to the heterosexual patriarchal world. The drawings depart from these ideas presenting idyllic situations in which the girl has opted for the opposite option remaining with the beast. I also introduce a different character who I call Queen Kong. Queen Kong is inspired by real woman that have chosen to live their sexualities in an unapologetic way.

Queen Kong expresses political incorrectness, lack of modesty and strength. In a world where women are being killed, they are in an endangered situation. This fact underlines the site specificity of the project having the whole planet as scenario. Paul Preciado emphasizes that we need to arm women. In the project's case the strength of Queen Kong in the drawings is replicated in the video installation where women and girls demonstrate their fighting skills. Queen Kong, the girls learn to fight presents the aesthetics of the politics of empowerment.

Elena Tejada-Herrera's drawings are autobiographical, they reflect hers and her friends' experiences. They respond to current events and are inspired by political and cultural events, questions of gender identity, issues of race and class problems, among others. Tejada-Herrera creates drawings in which she metaphorically represents situations of human relationships as inter-species relationships, normalizing the fluidity of gender and identities, with an irreverent, sexual and playful character. Feminism, queer and counter-colonial expressions are reflected in the different series. As Edouard Glissant said in his Poetics of Relation: "Relationships are multilingual and oppose the totalitarianism of a monolingual vision". The drawings of Elena TejadaHerrera are a performance of relationships and the exercise of the multilingual.

## Señorita de buena presencia buscando empleo – 1997

During the Fujimori dictatorship, Elena bursts into a round-table discussion at the First Lima Ibero-American Biennial in an outfit made of clippings from a newspaper's help-wanted section. The costume only exposed her crotch, calling attention to the Peruvian labor market's reigning racial and sexual authoritarianism.

## Bomba And The Bataclana In The Belly Dance—1994

The action was carried out in a local under the Center of Lima, the now extinct El Averno. She invited the passers-by from Quilca Street to enter the space in which she, dressed as a street or poor circus vedette, danced next to Bomba, a street artist who performed daily to earn a living, a "performance dance" being a woman with balloons that simulated gigantic breasts and buttocks. Both characters made a grotesque and hilarious musical/circus show that included the laceration of the breast prosthesis by her. The video of this action, which brought together popular culture, self-confidence and a sense of humor, gave Tejada-Herrera the first prize in the Passport for an Artist contest, which included a trip and residence in France.

Alfredo Villar

Nouveau Bourgeois Latin American Immigrant Who Learned Shopping and Has Good Taste is an action the artist performed for a number of years in various cities in the states of Virginia and New York. The performance's documentation begins with a four-channel projection. Elena Tejada-Herrera appears, standing and evincing an affirmative gesture at the entrance to a Williamsburg shopping center, brandishing a small sign that reads "Nouveau Bourgeois Latin American Immigrant Who Learns Shopping and Has Good Taste." The artist is dressed in garments that call up clichéd, vintage femininity: pearls, colorful skirts or dresses and high-heeled shoes. Her apparel's codification as stereotypically bourgeois contrasts with the evidently more popular origins of passers-by, who constitute an audience for the effects of this action. Immediately thereafter, the artist appears "in character" at different cultural institutions in the college town of Norfolk, also in Virginia, performing interventions at concerts, art galleries and cocktail bars. Finally the video takes us to New York's Union Square. On camera and in front of passers-by, Tejada-Herrera changes outfits several times, as if experimenting with the various identities she can take on through clothing. In general, the video has an ironic tone and presents the artist as selfconscious, reclaiming bourgeois origins, spending power and good taste, all the characteristics that do not correspond to the typical image of the Latino immigrant in the US public sphere.

Burping Wonderwoman was performed at Gallery Sensei and at China Town in the Summer of 2006. Burping Wonderwoman was influenced by the catastrophic events of 9/11 and its consequences. Thus, it has been facts like the terrorist attack of 9/11 and its repercusions, such as the Patriot Act what has influenced this artwork the most. I chose the character of Wonder-Woman because it is an icon of the American culture. Moreover it refers and responds to the law mentioned above that in a way restrains and constrains certain freedoms of expression. Her clothes could refer to the flag without being the flag itself. It is a complex work with multiple levels of meaning. There is the reference to the cultural shock I experienced as a new immigrant in the USA. There is the gender commentary using humor and challenging gender conventions. Burping Wonderwoman is an artwork of appropriation I realized as an immigrant. It emphasizes from my perspective as an immigrant and as a member of a subaltern group my relation to mainstream forms of the dominant cultures. For this I used forms and actions generated by dominant cultures. In that sense it talks more about the relation between periphery and center, between dominant cultures and subaltern cultures. I use humor to subvert a cultural icon. I do this from a post-colonial perspective. Humor affords the piece accessibility. Humor makes accessible subjects that could be controversial. Humor brings the artwork closer to the public in order to communicate discourses that emphasize problematic situations and conflicts. On the other side the piece is also a conversation within the context of art history with other emblematic art pieces. The public transfers her/his own information to analyze the artwork. The public resignifies it according to her/his own cultural individual particular background. The video of the performance in Chinatown is posted in my youtube page.

Elena Tejada-Herrera invited two tango teachers and a friend to dance tango and play soccer. The performance displays de process learning tango and practicing soccer. In the performance the rules are broken because within the traditional tango the man leads and the woman follows. Here there are only women dancing and exchanging roles. This is following a new popular practice in Argentina called Tango queer. The rules of soccer are also taken very loosely. The music displays pieces that go from proto-tango (which precedes tango) to electronic tango, after Astor Piazzolla's formal innovations. Elena interviewed people at milongas, and was welcomed by Argentinians with a lot of warm. Thus she learned about the relationship between the history of tango and the history of social movements and their relation to tango in Argentina. This is not present in the video, but it was a source of inspiration to understand the nature of tango steps.

The video was created and edited by Elena Tejada-Herrera.

## The Teasing Monkey Is Back: Interactive participative performance installation-2013

I currently am experimenting with media art technologies and their potential for the development of forms of social practice. For this I am building virtual landscapes and characters for the creation of participative interactive narratives with the public. These works are an invitation to the public to take an active role, to position themselves within these narratives, and to alter them. This is also an invitation to question established cultural conceptions as those presented by the mainstream media. I freely use different design tools, like hardware and software to build these environments and to freely create the images for the public to interact with. An example of this kind of work was the piece I presented in my thesis exhibition for which I built an immersive environment in which real time editing and video feedback allowed for the mixing of the public images and actions within the video projection. The installation was a queer space that allowed for queer time experiences.

Elena Tejada-Herrera
