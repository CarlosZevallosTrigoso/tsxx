La obra *Florecen luces sobre nuestras pieles* de Javi Vargas constituye un punto de inflexión en el discurso académico y artístico contemporáneo. Este libro es el resultado de un arduo trabajo de compilación y reflexión que abarca desde 1996 hasta 2023. Funciona como una especie de dispositivo polinizador de ideas, en el sentido de que las partículas conceptuales y visuales que libera tienen la capacidad de germinar en múltiples direcciones, fomentando así nuevas formas de pensar y ver en un mundo que, de muchas maneras, se enfrenta a desafíos apocalípticos tanto ecológicos como sociales.

El texto se articula en torno a varias líneas de investigación y creatividad que han sido constantes en la obra de Vargas. Una de estas es la recontextualización de figuras y símbolos heroicos indígenas. Esta reapropiación y reinterpretación de la historia y la mitología indígena apunta a una forma de resistencia cultural que va más allá de la mera representación; busca, en cambio, actualizar estos símbolos y dotarlos de relevancia en el contexto contemporáneo.

Otra línea de trabajo presente en el libro es la exploración de prácticas ancestrales que tocan temas como la astronomía y la relación del ser humano con la naturaleza. A través de este lente, Vargas ahonda en las concepciones culturales que informan nuestras percepciones del mundo y cómo estas, a su vez, configuran nuestra experiencia del mismo. La intertextualidad es una de las características más destacadas de esta obra. Vargas no solo establece un diálogo con figuras del arte y la literatura, sino que también lo entrelaza con temas sociopolíticos y ecológicos. Entre los interlocutores que podríamos encontrar están desde César Moro y Dina Paucar hasta Frida Kahlo y José María Arguedas. En este sentido, la obra se convierte en un palimpsesto cultural en el que diferentes discursos y símbolos se superponen y entrecruzan, creando una textura rica y compleja.

Con respecto a la identidad, el enfoque de Vargas se inclina hacia una celebración de la pluralidad y la complejidad. Lejos de buscar categorías fijas o identidades monolíticas, su obra aboga por la multiplicidad y la ambigüedad. Es un llamado a considerar la identidad como algo fluido y en constante evolución, más que como un conjunto de características inmutables.

En última instancia, *Florecen luces sobre nuestras pieles* no es un libro que mire hacia atrás con nostalgia, sino que plantea preguntas cruciales sobre el futuro. Se trata de un texto que invita al lector a reconsiderar lo que significa imaginar nuevas posibilidades en un mundo donde las opciones parecen cada vez más limitadas. Es, en resumen, una obra académica y artística que se posiciona como una fuente de profunda reflexión y debate y que indudablemente continuará generando conversaciones y estudios en los años venideros.

# Letter to Javi Vargas, to His Feathers, to His Sea of Lights That Will Never Go Out Miguel A. López

#### Dear Javi,

I am writing to you almost four weeks after the inauguration of *Florecen* luces sobre nuestras pieles [The Lights Blossom On Our Skins] in Lima. I am sending this letter from Toronto, where I moved a few days after the opening. I can't imagine a better farewell than having worked with you on this project, which has elicited such affection and admiration from the hundreds of friends who were eager to see more of your images: volcanic eruptions of tenderness and disobedience. You don't know how much I appreciate the effort you put into making this project possible during a time in your life that was, and still is, so difficult for you given your fragile state of health. I am grateful to Gala Berger, who helped us install a truly luminous, moving exhibition; and to all those who selflessly offered their hands and their hearts for whatever you needed of them. It was an impressive demonstration of love from all the lives you have touched. I can't help getting emotional when saying so; my eyes and my words tremble as I write to you. I wonder if my letter will prove up to the task of encompassing a poetics such as yours, so undomesticated, capable of opening up black holes and altering the gravitational movement of our bodies.

I feel like your work takes part in the world like the pollen in spring: millions of microscopic grains move freely about, causing new forms of life to germinate upon contact. That itinerant pollen causes fields of flowers grow; it shimmers in the dark and seductively penetrates the skin, turning one's hair into feathers and one's tongue into serpents. In the midst of the night's tenderness, your images flicker with the force of that which knows itself capable of jolting a planet on the path to extinction.

I am fascinated by the exodus that can be perceived in your works: a movement from the earth to the constellations, delving into the way the stars permit us to reconnect with a vagabond desire, a desire that seeks to escape all forms of surveillance. I find it hard to explain the spell that your images and poems cast on me. It's as if you were inviting us to take part in an erotic encounter with the past and the future, with the world above and the world below. One of the keys to understanding your work is the way in which it constantly blurs the boundaries between art and activism, dismantling their domesticated codes and the traditional expectations of how a political declaration should look in the world. That collapsing and contamination of your artistic/activist tendencies has gone against the prevailing consensus in the cultural field since the late nineties, a time when the imposition of neoliberalism started to produce an art scene that became increasingly adapted to the rhythms of business, the market, and a global industry shaped by entertainment.

Your earliest public artistic incursions were as a member of the Aguaitones collective (1998-2003), a group of students from different art schools who came together under the guidance of the architect María Burela to take a position and reclaim the public space that had been hijacked by the Fujimori dictatorship. The collective first appeared in 1998 with a mural created in collaboration with the National Human Rights Coordinator's Office, which was quickly censored and partially erased. But it is the second action that I find incredibly significant, marking as it did a point of confluence between art, popular demands, and a working-class feminist and peasant woman activism. In 1999, alongside the Movimiento Amplio de Mujeres (Women's Broad Front Movement, or MAM, founded in 1996), Aguatoines staged "Proyecto de mujer" ["Woman Project"], a protest and intervention that took place in Lima, in front of the Ministry of Health. On the sidewalk, the collective erected a number of sculptures sawn from plywood and then painted, dramatizing the thousands of operations performed on peasant and indigenous women without their consent, with phrases that explicitly revealed the patriarchal racism inherent in these events: «If you can't pay, we're not going to stitch you up, hon.» One of the largest elements was a vertical piece of fabric featuring the recurring image of a nurse calling for silence with her finger on her lips, in a clear allusion to the impunity of those who carried out these acts.

The MAM brought together a wide array of working-class women and neighborhood leaders, lesbian feminists, and a range of other organizations against the dictatorship, from the Asociación de Mujeres de la Provincia de Huancabamba (Huancabamba Province Women's Association, or AMHBA)—hailing from one of the areas most severely affected by the forced sterilization program—to the Movimiento de Mujeres Ciudadanas del Cono Norte (Cono Norte Women's Movement). In addition to the signs and sculptures, flyers were distributed containing explicit demands: «higher salaries for the country's health care workers; adequate infrastructure for medical services; and free care for childbirth and all related complications.» I believe this was the first artistic/activist manifestation to address the forced sterilizations that had been occurring since 1996,

While this early intervention sought to twist the hurtful power of invective through reappropriation (or deprivation) of the insult as a form of political declaration, "Letanías doradas" and "Letanías falladas" emphasize not only the word, but the way it is engraved on the body. In these videos, there is an exploration of the violence and convulsions that invective perpetrates against the body by affixing it within an offensive, essentialist categorization that is not always easy to undo. In these two videos, the strategy is modified: There is an exploration of the word «mostacero» and the animal taxonomy so frequently found in homophobic slurs in Peru (such as «billy goat,» «duck,» «butterfly,» and even «hen,» used as an accusation of masculine cowardice, with feminizing effects), this time with invective constructed using the body via the materiality of mustard and feathers. It is a way of rewriting invective through the brushing together of bodies, in an attempt to construct other rhetorics through insult, and to speak to the intersections between the terrorist and the queer body.

The use of mustard, in its twofold reference to food and feces, alludes to human life in its most basic dimension, as a continuity of the animal. Likewise, the use of feathers (which also bring to mind trans adornment and feminization) envelops the bodies in the morphology of birds.
