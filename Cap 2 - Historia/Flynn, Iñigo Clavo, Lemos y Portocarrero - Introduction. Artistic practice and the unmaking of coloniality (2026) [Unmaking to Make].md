Introduction: Artistic practice and
the unmaking of coloniality
Alex Ungprateeb Flynn, María Iñigo Clavo, Beatriz Lemos
and Florencia Portocarrero

‘Decolonising’ work has become a fixture in the world of contemporary
art. In Latin America, this shift gained momentum during the Bicentennial
celebrations of Independence, moments that catalysed a renewed critical
examination of the continent’s global legacies. Ecuador marked its bicen-
tennial in 2009, followed by Argentina, Mexico, Colombia and Chile in
2010, Peru in 2021, and Brazil in 2022. The artworks produced in rela-
tion  to  these  celebrations,  often  critically  responding  to  their  triumph-
alist  and  colonialist  framing,  became  a  point  of  inflection,  challenging
official narratives of nation- building and exposing the systematic exclu-
sion of Indigenous and Afro- diasporic communities from state histories –
histories frequently shaped by processes of racialised ‘whitening’. These
interventions did more than critique the past: they transformed the very
ways in which independence, memory and collective belonging could be
imagined in the present. A conspicuous lack of commemoration in Spain,
for example, was the starting point for Peruvian artist Sandra Gamarra
Heshiki’s solo exhibition Buen Gobierno (Good Government) in Madrid
in  2021  (see  Figure  0.1).  Addressing  the  neo- colonialism  that  prevails
in Spain’s relationship with Latin America, Gamarra put forward a series
of uncomfortable juxtapositions through contemporary and colonial- era
works and eventually complied with a request from the Culture Ministry
of  Madrid  to  remove  the  words  ‘racism’  and  ‘restitution’  from  the  wall
text at the exhibition’s entrance.

Such initiatives found resonance more globally. In 2014, Paul Preciado

Barcelona  (MACBA)  entitled  ‘Decolonising  the  Museum’  which  invited

and how these might challenge imperial and Eurocentric narratives and

Cecil Rhodes, sparking a global movement to decolonise universities and
their curricula, which in turn inspired groups like Decolonize This Place
to take actions at New York museums, including the Brooklyn Museum
and  the  Whitney.  The  publication  of  the  restitution  report  by  Felwine
Sarr and Bénédicte Savoy in 2018, which recommended the restitution
of African artefacts in French museums, was another significant moment.
Books such as Dan Hicks’ The Brutish Museums1 sparked a wave of public
debate  and  created  a  shift  in  public  perception,  which  began  to  place
pressure on archives, museums and cultural institutions to move toward
greater transparency regarding provenance, and in the contemporary art
world, a reappraisal of what kind of artworks were being shown and how
they should be contextualised.

In  the  present  context,  what  was  once  a  peripheral  position  has
today become an all- encompassing point of reference. The call to decol-
onise has become a powerful force, a rallying cry, and at times, a strategic
means to bring about public programmes, events and exhibitions in con-
temporary art. Much has been lost in this process. What was once an ex-
perimental and speculative programme has moved towards becoming an
institutional norm, so much so that Andrea Carlson has commented that
‘without naming names, museums with the splashiest displays of decol-
onization are often hiding the most bodies to this day’.2 Such has been the
force of the zeitgeist that even Frieze, the art fair behemoth, was moved
to commission a project entitled ‘Decolonial Documents’ in which artists,
curators  and  writers  discussed  ‘the  projects  that  have  informed  their
thinking around decolonizing culture’. In a classic case of awkward juxta-
position, almost simultaneously with the initiative’s publication, Frieze’s
inaugural  2018  Los  Angeles  edition  was  welcoming  Hollywood  actors,
studio  executives,  and  a  well-heeled  public  to  its  Paramount  Pictures
Studios location and charging exhibitors over $70,000 for an 861 square
foot booth.

It is easy to lean towards scepticism and poke fun at the contradictions
inherent  to  art.  But  an  unease  and  a  suspicion  that  attempts  to  decol-
onise  are  merely  a  box  to  be  checked  in  the  contemporary  art  world
apply  in  equal  measure  to  the  academy.  Although  the  decolonial  turn
has come to influence disciplines such as art history, anthropology and
the  fine  arts  among  others,  for  many  scholars  there  has  been  a  lack  of
structural progress with regard to tangible change. In addressing, for ex-
ample,  questions  of  which  epistemological  perspectives  are  considered
valid, or the diversity of tenured faculty and the kinds of students that are
admitted, there remains much to be done. It is noteworthy that in their
seminal  2012  essay  ‘Decolonization  is  not  a  metaphor’,  Eve  Tuck  and
K. Wayne Yang warn of the ‘easy adoption of decolonizing discourse by
educational advocacy and scholarship’3 in lieu of meaningful change. And
such intransigence should not be surprising. As Kehinde Andrews notes,
‘universities are institutionally racist spaces that have had an historic role
in producing the knowledge that racism is based on’.4 Negotiating such
complexities and seeking to write about them from within the academy is
a challenge. Brook Andrew, a Wiradjuri/ Celtic artist from Australia and
the artistic director of the 22nd Biennale of Sydney, has written of how
the academy silences perspectives it cannot assimilate, ‘waving a finger,
set in its ways, making nonsense of something not written in its dominant
ways’.5  Andrew’s  essay  is  part  of  a  special  issue  in  the  journal  October
entitled  ‘A  Questionnaire  on  Decolonization’,  and  in  the  same  volume,
Nitasha  Dhillon  comments  that  the  impulse  to  decolonise,  equally
underway in the arts as it is in academia, merits caution, ‘given the role
that these institutions generally play in maintaining the status quo’.6

Such concerns are entirely valid. And yet, when we as editors began
to conceive of this project in 2020, we felt there was a need to offer a re-
joinder to the increasingly superficial and problematic forms of engage-
ment  with  decolonisation  occurring  in  North  American  and  European
art  spaces.  Over  recent  years,  that  conviction  has  only  grown  as  the
production  of  the  discourse  of  decolonisation  has  become  ever  more
monopolised by the institutions of the Global North –  whether pertaining
to the arts or academia –  and such discourse’s relationship to artistic prac-
tice has shifted from one of theoretical inspiration to mere illustration.
One of our central contentions in this book is that artistic practice, with
its  generative  and  experimental  potential  of  imagining  the  world  and
proposing modes of being within it, cannot be reduced to simply making
visible and legible theories produced by the academy. In conceptualising
this volume, however, we were also aware of the importance of speaking
to a context without reifying it. While we were increasingly dismayed by

over again. Instead, we sought to base the volume and its contributions
on epistemological positions that exist outside the North American and
European canon and are often rendered invisible by that corpus of know-

ask  what  kinds  of  knowledges  such  artistic  practices  might  enable  and
enact,  what  kinds  of  institutions  they  might  make  possible,  and  what
types of languages and futures they might gesture towards.

Our  aim  with  this  book  therefore  is  to  reorient  the  theoretical
horizon  back  towards  Latin  America,  paying  specific  attention  to  arts
practitioners  working  within  contexts  of  coloniality.  As  editors,  we  are
deeply rooted within this context and believe that Latin American artistic
practice, characterised by its profound imbrication with sociality and a re-
fusal to compartmentalise aesthetics and politics, holds profoundly trans-
formative potential. Working with such practices is to foreground modes
of experimentation that prefigure otherness and theorise the everyday,
proposing a radical rereading of the colonial imposition. Rereading and
resignification are of course important, but we understand art’s theorising
potential  to  go  beyond  this.  Ailton  Krenak,  the  celebrated  Indigenous
writer and activist, has noted that art conjures the ‘possibility for us to
create worlds, [to] invent worlds for us to exist in’ and suggests that art
offers perhaps the most promising space for generating new enquiry to
shape the world moving forward.7 And it is this interlinked capacity to
both theorise how to resist and dismantle the aims of colonial regimes, as
well as to actively seek to bring this about, that characterises the extra-
ordinary  trajectories  of  artists  and  art  practitioners  across  the  length
and breadth of the Latin American continent. In our understanding, art-
istic practice is both a series of site- specific interventions as well as the
starting point for plural theoretical frameworks that encompass, but ul-
timately go beyond, epistemic rereadings. Contrary to Walter Mignolo’s
critique  of  historical  figures  within  the  anticolonial  tradition  –   which
suggests  that  they  were  unable  to  find  ‘an  opening  and  a  freedom  of
an  other  thinking’8  –   artistic  practice  undoubtedly  enunciates  the  as-
yet- unknown  and  finds  expression  via  both  the  body  and  our  shared
imaginaries to address racism, displacement and dispossession, and the
colonial extractivism that threatens our world.

In  this  manner,  we  address  the  fundamental  and  pioneering  role
of art in theorising, modelling and enacting plural modes of dismantling
colonial  regimes.  Through  17  contributions,  the  volume  encompasses

compact,  and  this  commitment  to  embodiment,  underlines  a  key  dis-
tinction in our approach. While much scholarly work details how artistic
projects are informed by, or otherwise illustrate, decolonisation as a pro-
ject, we work to manifest how Latin American artistic practice theorises
and articulates plural modes of being that are dynamically reshaping the
canon.  Throughout,  the  book’s  chapters  demonstrate  how  artistic  and
curatorial perspectives, especially those of Indigenous and Afro- diasporic
practitioners,  understand  art  as  a  reflexive,  methodological  practice,  a
modality of open- ended enquiry that anticipates and prefigures, located
in forms of living, doing, and knowing. Contributing to debates on de-
colonisation as Eurocentric project, the pluriverse and colonial tempor-
ality,  the  book  puts  forward  four  frames  through  which  to  understand
art’s  centrality:  counter- genealogies;  institutions;  the  decolonisation  of
language; and futures. What kind of interventions do arts practitioners
that sit outside canonical North American and European perspectives on
art history make to critical social enquiry? How can we conceptualise the
reflexive practice of Latin American institutions given the colonial matrix
in which their work is situated? What are the languages that artists pro-
vide for thinking about the relation between objects and spectators, com-
munities and knowledge, theory and practice? How can such languages
prefigure tomorrow’s modes of resistance to colonial regimes, and by ex-
tension, what might Latin American art as theoretical corpus contribute
to future global debates?

such questions, but also maintained an embodied commitment to them
in our engagement with exhibition- making and curatorial practice more
broadly. Maria Lind sketches curatorial practice (as opposed to curating
as a technical function) as something that encompasses critique, editing,

texts on art and museum studies that are written from North American
and  Eurocentric  perspectives  and  working  through  such  processes  has

styles, specific spellings and terms that are often untranslatable outside
their native languages, our methodology in creating this volume speaks to
our embodied commitment to the issues at stake.

Whether  it  is  desirable  to  make  available  a  collection  of  such
perspectives for an English language audience is a different question. We
note that our book is part of a wider trend of recognising arts practitioners
who are from what Europe and North America often consider the ‘per-
iphery’.  The  most  obvious  recent  example  is  the  2024  Venice  Biennale
Foreigners Everywhere, but there have been many others. Such inclusion
is often connected to fears surrounding the climate emergency, despair
at extractivist logics or uncertainty regarding our human relationship to
nature.  We  are  aware  that  the  presence  of  the  perspectives  we  articu-
late in this book may be considered merely a passing phenomenon by the
wider  art  world  and  international  curatorial  sphere,  just  like  anything
else arising from the logics of capitalism. And we understand that there is
a long history of how coloniality has appropriated the theoretical work of
those it seeks to contain. We envisage this collection therefore as an open-
access repository, not as an attempt to gain space, but rather as a form
of interlocution, a starting point for further conversations. The editorial

Directions of travel

The Latin American academy and its diaspora have been central to inter-
rogating the colonial present. A corpus of scholarship, developed by Latin
American academics including Walter Mignolo, Aníbal Quijano, Ramón
Grosfoguel, Arturo Escobar, Enrique Dussel, María Lugones and Nelson
Maldonado- Torres, has achieved widespread resonance since the 1990s,
coming  to  be  known  as  decolonial  theory.  Formulated  by  the  Grupo
Modernidad  /   Colonialidad  /   Decolonialidad  (hereafter  Grupo  MCD),
this  framework  seeks  to  uncover  and  challenge  a  persistent  coloniality
embedded  within  Western  modernity.  Modernity  and  coloniality  in
this  reading  are  understood  as  co- constitutive,  and  the  group’s  ana-
lysis  reaches  back  500  years  to  demonstrate  how  successive  phases
of  European  imperial  knowledge  practices  have  been  imposed  upon
subordinated and racialised non- European bodies and histories. Drawing
on foundational concepts such as Quijano’s ‘coloniality of power’10 and
Mignolo’s ‘epistemic delinking’, the corpus presents a sustained critique
of  knowledge  production,  questioning  and  denaturalising  European
knowledge systems and rejecting their claims of universality or inherent
superiority.11 The Grupo MCD, and Mignolo particularly, were reacting
to  the  intellectual  movement  of  Postcolonialism,  commonly  associated
with  the  ideas  of  Edward  Said,  Homi  Bhabha  and  Gayatri  Spivak.12

Decolonial scholars argue that there are many differences between the
two platforms, and both Gurminder Bhambra13 and Colpani et al.’s special
issue  in  the  journal  Postcolonial Studies  offer  useful  analyses,  weighing
such  claims.14  One  critique  from  the  Grupo  MCD  is  that  postcolonial
theory speaks from an Anglophone, literary tradition, which decolonial
scholars have criticised as reproducing a  universalising and imperialist
worldview. Grosfoguel particularly has claimed that postcolonial theory
‘departs from the monotopic and monologic practice of the Eurocentered
episteme’,15 with its dependence on thinkers like Foucault, Derrida and
Lacan, whereas the decolonial project ‘de- links’ from the Western canon
and  its  epistemological  assumptions.  In  this  line  of  argument  there-
fore, decolonial scholars claim that their approaches transcend Western
thinking, rendering postcolonial theory as elitist, overly theoretical and
standing outside of complex political realities.

While  the  Grupo  MCD  has  articulated  its  stance  and,  within  the
academy  at  least,  has  largely  succeeded  in  becoming  the  dominant
framework for theorising the ongoing colonial legacy in Latin America,
it  has  also  been  the  subject  of  criticism.  Michaelsen  and  Shershow,
for  example,  have  critiqued  the  inclination  of  the  group  to  idealise  or
generalise  non- Western  cultures,  often  merging  distinct  histories  and
struggles  in  a  manner  that  diminishes  their  particularity  and  unique
nuances.16  The  Aymara  sociologist  Silvia  Rivera  Cusicanqui  has  also
described the group’s work as an ‘unintelligible, elitist’ intellectual pro-
ject that is ‘practically useless for action in the streets and for engaging
with concrete Indigenous struggles’.17 Accusing Mignolo of establishing
‘an  empire  within  an  empire’,  Cusicanqui  argues  that  decolonial
theorists have created an intellectual asymmetry of power with regard
to the Indigenous communities they repeatedly draw inspiration from.18
A particularly persistent critique centres on the Grupo MCD’s tendency
to  incorporate  theorists  into  their  ever  expanding  body  of  literature,
claiming them post hoc as part of the decolonial platform. Colpani et al
cite Maldonado- Torres’ capacious definition of the decolonial turn19 and
call attention to how Maldonado- Torres manages to include figures such
as  W.E.B.  Du  Bois,  Aimé  Césaire,  Frantz  Fanon,  Sylvia  Wynter,  Gloria
Anzaldúa,  Lewis  Gordon,  Chela  Sandoval  and  Linda  Tuhiwai  Smith,
among  others,  in  the  Grupo  MCD’s  theoretical  canon.20  Such  a  ten-
dency  to  appropriate  practitioners  with  little  or  no  connection  –   par-
ticularly  with  regard  to  Black  feminist  thought  and  women  of  colour
feminisms –  has been widely commented on. Aura Cumes has pointed
out  how  the  decolonial  theorisations  of  non- Indigenous  thinkers  can
‘subordinate and subalternise our own struggles, thoughts and words’;21

Perhaps it is significant that the MACBA symposium ‘Decolonising
the  Museum’,  an  event  based  primarily  within  contemporary  art  as
opposed  to  the  academy,  chose  to  take  its  theoretical  lead  from  anti-
colonial  practitioners  such  as  Frantz  Fanon,  Angela  Davis  and  Aimé
Césaire, rather than the Grupo MCD. A key concern for the symposium’s
participants  was  how  coloniality  is  traversed  by  class  struggle  and
sexual oppression, aspects that perhaps those present felt had been left
undertheorised by the decolonial scholars. Grupo MCD is composed of
various scholars, and while Walter Mignolo has been subject to specific
critiques,  the  group  does  not  pretend  to  put  forward  a  unified  body  of
theory.  Arturo  Escobar,  for  example,  has  worked  ethnographically  for
decades with Afro- Colombian communities, and is recognised as an im-
portant scholar and activist. That said, practitioners in the contemporary
art world have found decolonial theory less pertinent than scholars in the
academy: of the artists contributing to this book, none have worked with
the decolonial framework in a longitudinal sense –  perhaps due to what
some  artists  have  identified  as  the  theory’s  rather  unidirectional  and
overdetermined approach.24 Although there have been attempts to con-
struct  an  alternative  platform  to  Western  aesthetics,25  these  initiatives
reveal  how  the  academy  often  turns  to  artistic  practice  to  illustrate  its
theoretical work. By contrast, what we articulate in this volume is how
the generative potential of open- ended and experimental art work goes
beyond such narrow constraints, resulting in the theorisation and articu-
lation of complex and unpredictable modes of being.

Such  unpredictability  is  a  cornerstone  of  the  pluriverse.  Marisol
de la Cadena and Mario Blaser have written of a world of many worlds,
where ‘heterogeneous worldings [come] together as a political ecology
of practices, negotiating their difficult being together in heterogeneity’.26
And  the  work  articulated  by  the  academy  since  the  1990s  to  theorise
ongoing  colonial  realities  in  Latin  America  is  becoming  increasingly
fragmented.  But  this  was  always  the  case.  From  beyond  the  academy,
theorists, practitioners, artists and activists have consistently produced

Counter- genealogies

In  the  late  1990s,  Linda  Tuhiwai  Smith  presented  her  seminal  work
Decolonizing  Methodologies  and  expressed  a  dilemma  in  relation  to  the
representation  of  history  and  time.  ‘Transforming  our  colonized  views
of our own history (as written by the West), however, requires us to re-
visit, site by site, our history under Western eyes. And yet, the need to tell
our stories remains the powerful imperative of a powerful form of resist-
ance’.32 Noting such contradiction, she continues:

We  live  simultaneously  within  (hegemonic)  views  while  needing
to pose, contest and struggle for the legitimacy of oppositional or
alternative histories, theories and ways of writing. … This means
struggling to make sense of our own world while also attempting to
transform what counts as important in the world of the powerful.
Part of the exercise is about recovering our own stories of the past.
This is inextricably bound to a recovery of our language and epis-
temological foundations. … [In brief] to hold alternative histories
is to hold alternative knowledges.33

is  remarkably  prescient  (see  Figure  0.2).  Comprised  of  a  tiny  wooden

cube (9 × 9 × 9 mm) made of pine and oak, the artwork explores the
idea  of  latent  energy  contained  within  minimal  forms.  Meireles  drew
from  Indigenous  knowledges  that  rubbing  a  piece  of  oak  against  pine
could create fire –  a fundamental human discovery.

The combination of these two woods serves as a metaphor for the
potential energy that exists in even the smallest of objects. When Meireles
installs  this  nearly  invisible  cube  on  the  floor  of  a  museum  space,  it
often  goes  unnoticed  –   a  commentary  on  how  diverse  knowledges  are
rendered invisible within the linear teleology of coloniality. However, the
cube’s  latent  combustibility  speaks  to  the  potential  of  such  Indigenous
knowledges to challenge and even metaphorically ‘ignite’ the structures
of oppression, in this case by setting the entire museum ablaze. In this
way, Cruzeiro do Sul complements subsequent theoretical developments,
pointing  to  how  Indigenous  knowledges  can  profoundly  impact  our
understanding and reshape the museum as a space for representing his-
tory and knowledge.

From  this  perspective,  and  taking  artistic  practice  as  its  starting
point,  Part  I  (Counter- genealogies)  aims  to  complexify,  engage  and  re-
spond  to  genealogies  of  decolonisation,  ranging  from  anticolonial
movements, to postcolonial theory, to the more recent decolonial turn.
