Reconsidering the ‘art museum’:
some reflections on the history of the
MALI collections
ricardo kusunoki and sharon Lerner

As  the  old  saying  goes,  ‘making  a  virtue  out  of  necessity’  has  been  the
main challenge faced by the Museo de Arte de Lima (MALI) since it first
opened its doors on 10 March 1961, in the heart of the historic centre
of the Peruvian capital. The institution was born out of the efforts of a
patronage set up by local businessmen and intellectuals, who sought to
remedy the paradoxical lack of an art museum in a country which, for
almost half a century, had been trying to define itself as a modern nation
precisely by highlighting its ancient artistic heritage. At that moment, it
was  indeed  impossible  to  incorporate  representative  revisions  of  much
of  the  art  developed  in  the  Peruvian  territory  into  the  new  museum’s
collections,  because  entire  groups  of  heritage  pieces  had  already  been
systematically  extracted  to  satisfy  the  demand  of  the  international
antiques market.1

MALI was not only a latecomer compared to the fine arts museums
opened  in  other  capitals  in  Latin  America,  such  as  Santiago  de  Chile
(1880),  Buenos  Aires  (1896)  and  Rio  de  Janeiro  (1937).  The  Lima  in-
stitution  was  also,  unlike  these  museums  which  emerged  in  close
connection  to  the  main  centres  of  artistic  education  in  their  respective
cities, not conceived as a carefully calculated part of a larger framework.
Added to this was the role played by chance in the configuration of MALI’s
founding collections. Rather than being the result of a systematic selec-
tion process, the collection of the museum in its first decades was made
up of the most diverse objects donated on the initiative of their founders.
That is why the main axis structuring these early collections was called

Memoria Prado (Prado Memory), an ambitious assemblage of more than
5000 diverse objects, including furniture, clothing, fans and coins, which
were  collected  by  jurist  and  historian  Javier  Prado  Ugarteche  (1871–
1921) at the start of the twentieth century.

Although it was largely defined by contingencies, the vast material
universe that made up the institution’s founding collections confronted
the  spectator  with  a  relatively  broad  definition  of  art  and  the  artistic.
The  collection  was  initially  organised  according  to  chronological  cri-
teria, divided into major periods that spanned from pre- Columbian times
to  early  modernity  –   a  categorisation  that  led  to  museographic  display
choices as well as to the current  organisation  into  curatorial  areas.  Yet
the works often escaped the strict demarcations of the academic canon
of the fine arts.2 This broad definition of artistic practice was doubtlessly
supported by a local scene that, from the 1920s on, had recognised the
full artistic condition of ‘their own’ pre- Hispanic or viceregal expressions
that  didn’t  correspond  to  the  international  canon  of  fine  arts.  But  the
eclectic grouping of all kinds of objects in an art museum was also pos-
sible  because  the  institution  was  independent  of  any  teaching  centres
and had no other instances of official validation of the arts at that time.

Indeed, the contemporary visual arts played a minor role in the his-
torical narrative, starting from ‘the dawn of civilisation’ and emphasising
the  truly  ‘Peruvian  character’  of  colonial  and  pre- Columbian  art,
compared to the present. This did not mean that MALI refused to support
the contemporary art scene. On the contrary, the institution became an
important  haven  for  contemporary  artistic  creation.  But  activating  the
possibilities  the  museum  offered  for  a  deeper  reflection  on  the  present
required  drawing  on  that  accumulation  of  time  and  the  materials  of
its  collections.  They  held,  in  latent  form,  the  possibility  of  questioning
how national narratives are projected onto the past while also enabling
a more distanced perspective on the self- referential rhetoric of modern
artistic discourses.3

Categories in tension

When  we  create  relations  between  the  material  horizons  of  different
periods and contexts, it fosters the idea that art possesses a universal char-
acter –  one that enables us to conceive of it as a continuous field, capable of
incorporating human differences. As if it were a projection of the current
ideal of a democratic public sphere, different visual expressions can be
installed  within  the  same  environment,  where  one  can  move  between

them on the same footing, beyond ethnic, cultural or social differences
that  separate  their  producers  or  consumers.  But  in  establishing  these
dialogues,  art  museums  may  risk  radically  homogenising  the  material
universe of the past – especially when they relegate to the background
the uses and functions that make any object something more than a mere
vehicle for aesthetic contemplation.

Navigating  the  two  sides  of  the  same  process  became  necessary
in 2017 when, thanks to the efforts of art historian Gabriela Germaná,
the  association  Con/ Vida  –   Popular  Arts  of  the  Americas  donated  the
series Piraq Causa (¿Quién es el causante?) (Who is the cause?) to MALI.
The series consists of 31 paintings that show scenes from life in Sarhua,
a community in Ayacucho –  one of the Andes regions most affected by
the  confrontations  between  terrorist  group  PCP- Sendero  Luminoso
and  the  Peruvian  military  during  the  1980s  and  1990s.  All  the  works
included  in  this  series  stem  from  the  collective  work  of  the  Asociación
de  Artistas  Populares  de  Sarhua  (Sarhua  Popular  Artists  Association,
ADAPS), formed by inhabitants of the community who emigrated to the
capital,  among  them  Primitivo  Evanán  Poma  and  Valeriana  Vivanco,
Juan  Walberto  Quispe,  Camelón  Berrocal  and  Julián  Ramos.  With  a
truly  exceptional  narrative  virtuosity,  the  series  unfolds  as  a  sequence
of scenes which present the records of communal memory. It is a story
that  describes  the  extreme  violence  which  the  terrorist  group  Sendero
Luminoso  unleashed  on  Sarhua,  the  abuse  perpetrated  by  the  Sinchis
(or soldiers), the resistance of the population and their final exodus to-
wards the capital. Piraq Causa should therefore not only be seen as a tes-
timony on behalf of the victims of violence, but it is also one of the most
sophisticated and moving visual statements of the period.4

The entry of that series into the MALI collection became a crucial
step  for  the  institution,  because  it  complexified  one  of  the  key  axes  of
the contemporary art collection then being developed: the narrative axis
constructed  around  the  so- called  ‘years  of  violence’.  Thus,  Piraq  Causa
was integrated –  without any major contradictions –  into an already ex-
istent  corpus  that  ranged  from  documentary  photography  to  pieces  by
artists  such  as  Eduardo  Villares,  Ricardo  Wiesse  and  the  group  Taller
N.N., works of a more conceptual nature and linked to activism and de-
nunciation.  However,  Piraq  Causa  introduced  a  genre  of  painting  that
still lacked representation in the museum collection, because it belongs
to  a  very  specific  creative  milieu  that  sits  between  rural  and  urban
contexts,  regional  tradition,  the  preservation  of  memory  and  contem-
porary production. In what constituted one of the most complex concep-
tual constructions of local modernism, the emergence and consolidation

of  an  autonomous  field  for  the  arts  assumed  the  form  of  two  parallel
discourses.  One  of  them,  so- called  ‘cultured  art’,  was  associated  with
academic  practice  and  the  modern  rhetoric  of  innovation  and  rupture.
However, the series donated to MALI belonged to the realm of ‘popular
art’, that is, the expression of a community whose cultural ‘authenticity’
is defined by the preservation of a ‘traditional’ universe of forms.

Rather  than  being  acknowledged  as  a  modern  creative  sphere,
‘popular art’ came to be understood as a category that traverses all historical
processes –  particularly insofar as the ‘people’ it claimed to represent were,
in  fact,  a  condensation  of  multiple  temporalities  and  diverse  social  and
ethnic realities. The concept itself was first formulated in the 1920s within
the  nationalist  perspective  promoted  by  painter  José  Sabogal,  who  used
the term ‘popular’ for artistic manifestations that did not conform to aca-
demic canons or that originated in the countryside. This was not, however,
a transparent gesture, since the term was associated with the idea of na-
tional authenticity whose axis was the mixing of ‘Indigenous’ and ‘Spanish’.
Furthermore,  many  regional  artistic  traditions,  in  the  process  of  being
acknowledged as ‘popular art’, modified their original modes of production
and their intended audience to become objects of consumption for a new
urban public with specific expectations about what was ‘their own’. Despite
its evidently modern condition, ‘popular art’ was thus considered an expres-
sion of a vernacular identity rooted in the past and opposed to the dynamics
of constant transformation typical of modernity and ‘cultured’ art.

Although  Piraq  Causa  consists  of  several  paintings,  they  are  not
exactly  considered  as  paintings.  The  correct  term  for  them  is  Sarhua
tablets, an artistic tradition that originated in the local custom of giving
roof beams as gifts to newlyweds for their homes. It is a practice that, as
Gabriela  Germaná  points  out,  probably  started  at  the  end  of  the  nine-
teenth century, when these beams were decorated with very schematic
portraits of those who donated them. When several residents of Sarhua
migrated  to  Lima  in  the  1970s,  they  replaced  the  production  of  these
beams with the painting of small tablets with narrative images of local
customs, now intended for urban collections and the tourist trade. It is no
coincidence that Piraq Causa came about through an explicit commission
by Swiss journalist Peter Gaupp, who asked Sarhua artists to work within
the  narrative  register  which  defined  them  as  ‘popular  artists’,  but  to
capture  the  memory  of  violent  times.  So,  although  they  do  not  have
the same appearance or function as their rural predecessors, the small
paintings –  with scenes from everyday life and customs –  that make up
the Piraq Causa are still called Sarhua tablets, preserving the powerful
aura of the ‘authentic’ that is associated with the ‘traditional’.

  In  fact,  the  founding  of  the  Museo  de  Arte  de  Lima  itself  can  be
seen as a key moment in the binary construction of the modern artistic
field.  This  did  not  prevent  the  institution  from  regularly  playing  a  key
role in the promotion of non- academic traditions on the urban market.
This  effort  reached  its  peak  with  the  Bienales  Nacionales  de  Artesanía
(National  Craft  Biennials),  held  in  1967  and  1969.  However,  these
initiatives  made  no  major  impact  on  the  institution’s  own  collections.5
It is noteworthy that the discrepancy between the historical collection,
characterised by its multiplicity of materials and origins, and the homo-
geneity of the more recent works was not evident. This situation began
to change notably thanks to the work of Natalia Majluf, who served as
general  curator  (1995– 2001) and later  director  (2001– 2018)  of MALI.
This  shift  reached  a  milestone  in  2015,  when  the  museum  established
a  narrative  axis  around  the  Amazon,  based  on  its  contemporary  art
collection.6 Back then, the Amazon region was already one of the local
scenes with the greatest agency and outreach, thanks to the actions of in-
dependent artists, researchers and curators.7 The museum’s commitment
to systematically represent the artistic production connected to this re-
gion  generated  a  broader  context  in  which  previous  acquisitions  could
be situated. In 2014, the first two works by an artist from the regional
Indigenous  communities  entered  the  collection:  they  were  two  large
scale  paintings  by  Murui  Bora  artist  Brus  Rubio  Churay  (Paucarquillo,
Loreto, 1984). At that time, there was only a single item in the collection
produced by those who identify and claim an Indigenous identity. This
was a set of Shipibo pottery, added to the institutional holding in 2001,
after it had been displayed in El ojo verde. Cosmovisiones amazónicas (The
Green  Eye.  Amazonian  Cosmovisions),  curated  by  Gredna  Landolt  at
Fundación Telefónica in 2001 and considered a landmark exhibition for
its contemporary view on Amazonian practice.

When the contemporary collection at MALI was initiated in 2008,
thanks to the creation of a special fund dedicated to that end, the mu-
seum had already contemplated the acquisition of a vast number of works
by artists who explored key themes about the Amazon, such as the social
conflict linked to the unbridled exploitation of its resources, the claims of
Indigenous communities in defence of their territories, and the elusive
appreciation  and  preservation  of  ancestral  traditions.  Collecting  based
solely on a single thematic axis –  just as with Piraq Causa and its relation-
ship to the period of violence –  allowed works by artists from Indigenous
communities  to  appear  alongside  works  by  artists  with  a  national  or
international academic background on equal terms. But it also meant si-
lencing the specificity of local artistic languages as cultural constructions

Projecting the past onto the present

The  incorporation  of  the  Piraq  Causa,  together  with  the  establishment
of an Amazonian axis, opened the way for MALI to critically reassess the
work it had carried out over the previous two decades in building its his-
torical  collections.  Throughout  this  period,  the  institution  took  up  the
programmatic and constant strengthening of its collections as one of its
main public functions. Its commitment was to recover a wider material
universe created before the twentieth century, based on two main prem-
ises. First, the lack of acquisition policies in most of Peru’s museums put
numerous key historical aspects of the region’s visual culture at risk of
dispersion or total disappearance. Second, the need to safeguard these
testimonies  rested  on  another  very  evident  assumption  premise,  espe-
cially for an art museum whose collections aspire to cover a time span
of thousands of years: namely, that the category of art as a differentiated
field  did  not  exist  for  most  of  that  period.  From  MALI’s  curatorial  per-
spective,  applying  such  a  category  to  earlier  historical  contexts  thus
implied  avoiding  any  rigid  differentiation  between  what  can  or  cannot
be  considered  art.  Instead,  the  aim  was  to  conceive  of  the  artistic  as  a
material universe organised by ever- shifting coordinates and defined by
gradations, rather than fixed boundaries.

Perhaps  the  main  potential  of  putting  together  a  contemporary
collection  was  the  possibility  that  a  fluid  universe  of  objects  from  the
past  would  cast  its  shadow  on  the  immediate  present,  to  reshape  it.
Within this logic, the incorporation  of Piraq Causa  or  the  creation of a
contemporary Amazonian collection should not simply be understood as
institutional  recognition  of  the  full  artistic  condition  of  a  creative  field
previously considered ‘popular art’ or ‘crafts’. In fact, these two concepts
do not only refer to hierarchical instances of valuation. The emergence
of the term ‘popular art’ is, in itself, an act of configuring a new creative
space, characterised by concrete expectations for both its consumers and
its producers. Thus, MALI’s institutional gesture must be understood as
part of the broader process of dismantling the dichotomous structure of
the Peruvian artistic field, one historically grounded in class and ethnic
divisions. Piraq Causa entered the institution not only as a work of art,
but also as popular art and craft, since these latter terms refer to historic-
ally determined coordinates of creation. Separating the work from those

terms to arbitrarily install it in the supposedly timeless space of the ‘truly’
artistic would be, although it may sound paradoxical, a way of validating
the fictions inherited from modernism. It is precisely the intersectional,
almost ambivalent, nature of the piece that presents us with its discursive
richness and full contemporary validity, which does not depend on any
pure essence or predetermined aesthetic categories.

Approaching the present through the past enables an ‘art museum’
to radically confront the semantic power of this term that defines its iden-
tity. In this way, from its desire to address a broad temporal horizon, the
institution aims to bring together a wide universe of objects considered to
be of great artistic importance because they have had the power to shape,
transform  or  question  local  societies,  whether  as  visual  constructions
or  material  presences.  This  understanding  of  the  artistic  must  be
distinguished  from  the  other  story  that  the  institution  also  aspires  to
tell: that of ‘art’ as a modern concept that sets boundaries to constitute
its own space of autonomy. Therefore, MALI takes on the task of trying
to  understand  the  meanings  and  social  connections  that  are  sustained
through the objects, rather than opting for a vindictive or essentialist at-
titude towards what should be considered ‘art’ within that differentiated
field. This should not be interpreted as an uncritical acceptance of all cat-
egories that define the artistic field at any given moment. On the contrary,
it implies recognising that these coordinates are historical constructions
that are always changing.

In  this  way,  the  multiplicity  of  objects  that  make  up  the  MALI
collections do not give rise to a transformation of the art museum into a
repository of material culture. Rather, they allow us to approach the very
formation of the category of art as a differentiated space –  one that draws
boundaries in a broader universe of objects and redefines what is within it.
For this reason, we have not defined the work of collecting as a task of res-
cuing ‘heterodox’, ‘popular’, or ‘mixed’ aesthetics, because validating these
categories only reaffirms an ideal of ‘purity’ that is counterproductive to
approaching any cultural process. It is precisely an art-historical perspec-
tive that, applied to a reading of the collection, allows us to confront other
essentialist topics related to key issues such as class or ethnicity.

The work of collecting strives for the very broad representation of
a population made up of very diverse groups, which are not necessarily
limited  to  geographical  boundaries  or  traditional  historical  narratives
of the nation-state. However, questioning this type of rhetoric must not
simply  lead  to  erecting  other  identitarian  constructions  that,  because
they  are  based  on  ethnic  considerations,  are  conceived  as  monolithic
truths in the same way as nationalist discourses.8

To avoid all essentialism, it is crucial to consider a concept such as
tradition –  understood as the genealogical line that, in any given period,
tracks  a  form  of  knowledge  or  a  repertoire  of  conventions.  Although
the notion evokes the much- used term ‘school’, here it reclaims its ori-
ginal  meaning  of  knowledge  transmitted  from  master  to  disciple,  be-
fore the word ended up meaning the expression of a national or ethnic
‘Kunstwollen’.9 Traditions set out multiple parallel lines that constantly
intersect, so that the spectrum of the artistic is defined as a multiplicity of
stories that are not always related to the artistic field as an autonomous
modern construction.

Enter multiplicity

From time to time, the guidelines that define the growth of the contem-
porary collection are revised, without assuming any prescriptive notions
about artistic value. And while the work of forming the collections and
the criteria used in curatorial discussions within the institution take into
account these discursive considerations and complexities regarding the
historical contextualisation of categories of the artistic, it is only in the
realm  of  public  presentation  –   through  exhibitions,  publications  and
programmes –  that they are truly realised.

Perhaps  the  first  and  most  important  moment  of  publicising  the
internal  work  processes  of  the  museum  took  place  in  2019.  The  exhib-
ition Amazonías was presented at the cultural centre Matadero in Madrid,
within the framework of the ARCO art fair. A complementary exhibition
was  held  in  Lima  simultaneously:  Otras  historias  posibles.  Repensando
las  colecciónes  del  MALI  (Other  Possible  Stories.  Rethinking  the  MALI
Collections).  Conceived  as  a  transversally  curated  exhibition,  Otras
historias posibles presented some preliminary lines of research that guided
the growth of the museum’s collections between 2010 and 2019. The ex-
hibition  included  different  thematic  groupings  that  emerge  from  the
collections, such as the textile tradition of the southern Andes, the photo-
graphic portrait as a mass genre, an early history of local graphic art, and
a  presentation  of  how  forms  typical  of  regional  traditions  transitioned
into  so- called  ‘popular  art’  and  ‘contemporary  art’.  The  exhibition  itself
was an institutional milestone for being a project that sought to highlight
historical discontinuities, rather than trace great ‘artistic’ genealogies.

A  few  years  later,  when  activities  in  the  museum  resumed  after
the COVID- 19 pandemic, the programmes carried out in the permanent
collection rooms on the second floor similarly aimed to reflect the change

been lacking in art-historical narratives or narratives about the collection.
Both presentations showed how the contemporary collection –  much like
the historical collections –  tried to move beyond the context of Lima by
incorporating the gaze of other regional scenes, without proposing a con-
temporary reification of the tendency to essentialise practices, stage ‘the
other’, or resort to the rhetoric of the ‘impure’.

Today, visitors to MALI will find that the narratives of the museum
seek to address two distinct qualities associated with the term art, in a
differentiated way. There is the narrative that explores different modal-
ities of visual creation from a perspective that values their aesthetic quality.
At the same time, the other narrative reflects on art as a constructed cat-
egory. In its aspiration to forge an autonomous space, art imposes a power
dynamic that draws boundaries in a much broader material universe. It is
impossible to confront the myths about art without exploring what is left
outside that field. In fact, considering something as art is not just an act of
naming. Categories such as ‘popular art’ or ‘Indigenous art’ cannot only
be understood as spaces where non- academic forms of visual production
are recognised. They are creative fields that, due to their ‘artistic’ nature,
also  have  their  own  boundaries.  As  a  result,  what  is  needed  is  a  broad
collections  policy  that,  by  reconstructing  constellations  of  objects  and
connections, can provoke the awareness needed for us to place ourselves
inside  a  set  of  coordinates  that  are  historically  situated,  and  therefore,
capable of being transformed.
