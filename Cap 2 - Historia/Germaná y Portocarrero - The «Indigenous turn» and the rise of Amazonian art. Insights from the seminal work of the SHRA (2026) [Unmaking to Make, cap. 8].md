8
The ‘Indigenous turn’ and the rise
of Amazonian art: insights from the
seminal work of the Seminario de
Historia Rural Andina (SHRA)
gabriela germaná and Florencia Portocarrero

I

For much of Peru’s republican history, Indigenous and vernacular artists,
along with their distinctive forms of aesthetic and cultural production,
have been excluded from official art history. The origins of this exclusion
can  be  traced  back  to  the  nineteenth  century,  when  the  concept  of  art
in  Peru  was  aligned  with  the  paradigms  of  Eurocentric  modernity  and
disseminated  –   through  museums  and  fine  arts  schools  –   according  to
Western aesthetic hierarchies.1

Indeed,  the  concept  of  art,  based  on  strong  European  narratives
and institutions and transplanted to Latin America, almost unchanged,
has operated as one of the most powerful and persistent devices of exclu-
sion of Indigenous creators, whose cultural expressions were relegated to
the category of crafts, generating a theoretical and ethical impasse that,
at least in the case of Peru, has marked the history of art until the present.
While  it  is  undeniable  that  the  dichotomy  between  art  and  crafts
exists  and  persists  in  other  contexts,  what  distinguishes  the  Peruvian
case is its deeply racialised nature. On one side of this duality, we find
European art, which claims to be universal, defining what is considered
art  in  terms  of  innovation,  aesthetics  and  cultural  value.  On  the  other
side, objects created by Indigenous authors, especially those that do not
imitate  Western  styles  or  lack  academic  training,  are  relegated  to  the
realm of the exotic and even the primitive. It is this polarising logic that,

until the end of the twentieth century in Peru, led to denial of the possi-
bility that something could be simultaneously art and Indigenous.

In  Lo  andino  en  el  arte  peruano2  (The  Andean  in  Peruvian  Art,
1980),  cultural  critic  Mirko  Lauer  argues  that  the  organisation  of  cul-
ture –  articulated around the idea that artistic production is only access-
ible to a ‘modern and erudite’ minority –  has moulded a deeply stratified
artistic  field,  characterised  by  the  coexistence  of  three  independent
and  highly  contrasted  aesthetic  universes:  ‘high  art’,  associated  with
the European fine arts tradition; the art of native and mestizo cultures,
relegated to the realm of crafts; and pre- Hispanic art, viewed as archaeo-
logical heritage. The author identifies that each of these universes has
an autonomous area of study –  art criticism, anthropology and archae-
ology, respectively –  as well as clearly delimited circuits of diffusion and
commercialisation:  the  gallery,  the  folk- art  market  and  the  archaeo-
logical museum.

Lauer affirms that this complex multipolar structure was reproduced
in other countries in the region, with Indigenous majorities remaining on
the margins of power. Throughout the text he identifies different moments
of rupture or fracturing of their hierarchies. A first key episode occurred
in  the  1920s,  when  the  artists  of  the  Indigenist  movement,  trained  in
the European fine arts, began to observe with interest the visual produc-
tion of the rest of the country, incorporating into their works elements
inspired  by  contemporary  vernacular  forms  and  the  pre- Hispanic  past.
Although  this  unprecedented  interest  in  the  Indigenous,  specifically  in
the Andean and rural world, represented a turning point in the history
of Peruvian art, the Indigenist artists’ tendency toward aestheticisation
from a telluric perspective and their idealisation of bucolic values often
contradicted their visual practice, which failed to delve deeper into the
‘aesthetic thinking of the dominated’.3 Moreover, as art historian Natalia
Majluf has stated, Indigenist artists abstained from working on ‘artisanal’
forms for fear of distancing themselves from the ‘high art’ narrative.4

On  the  other  hand,  as  historians  Gabriela  Germaná  and  Amy
Bowman- McElhone have pointed out, the Indigenists played a key role
in  creating  the  category  of  arte  popular  (popular  art)  by  studying  and
promoting the works of rural and native artists in Lima.5 This initiative,
which  sought  to  establish  the  visual  arts  of  the  ‘people’  as  the  founda-
tion and bastion of a national art history, failed to dismantle the aesthetic
hierarchies and social inequalities that underpinned the distinction be-
tween  art  and  craft.  However,  it  did  manage  to  partially  attenuate  the
sense  of  identity  alienation  that  the  local  artistic  milieu  had  inherited
from its academic training in the European fine arts.

Another  important  milestone  took  place  in  the  1970s,  during
the  Gobierno  Revolucionario  de  las  Fuerzas  Armadas  (Revolutionary
Government  of  the  Armed  Forces,  GRFA)  of  General  Juan  Velasco
Alvarado  (1968– 1975),  when  crafts  were  politically  redefined  and
positioned  as  a  central  component  of  national  culture.6  This  effort  had
two main features. Firstly, the Premio Nacional de Fomento a la Cultura
(National  Prize  for  the  Promotion  of  Culture)  was  awarded  to  Joaquín
López  Antay,  a  retablista  (image  maker)  from  Ayacucho,  in  1975  –   a
symbolic gesture that laid the foundations for the emergence of a select
group of Andean personalities who began to produce their work under an
‘artistic logic’, positioning themselves as figures with their own name and
thus  breaking  with  the  historical  anonymity  that  had  preceded  them.7
Secondly,  a  plan  was  implemented  to  transform  the  political  economy
of  folk  art,  integrating  the  artisan  into  a  guild  –   the  artisanate  –   with
intellectual  property  rights  over  their  creations  and  rights  over  profits
generated without intermediaries. This policy had a significant impact on
the expansion of the circuits of craft diffusion, but it also implied the ‘pro-
letarianisation of a collective in relation to its own artistic production’.8

More recently, during the 1990s, the so- called global ‘Indigenous
turn’ marked a crucial change. During much of the twentieth century and
in  the  face  of  the  various  agrarian  reforms  that  took  place  throughout
Latin  America,  class  was  the  privileged  framework  for  understanding
Indigenous identity.9 Thus, native peoples were understood as peasants
and,  therefore,  defined  by  features  such  as  rural  poverty,  subsistence
agriculture  and  union  organisation.10  However,  during  the  1980s,  the
focus on social class began to lose relevance as Indigenous communities
adopted  new  identities  as  First  Nations,  claiming  historical  rights  over
the  territories  they  had  inhabited  since  ancestral  times.  This  process,
facilitated in part by the work of anthropologists and non- governmental
organizations (NGOs), coincided with the rise of neoliberal multicultur-
alism, positioning Indigeneity as a central category that allowed commu-
nities to articulate and claim rights both locally and internationally.11

In the cultural sphere, this shift has generated a slow but sustained
opening  of  contemporary  art  circuits  to  Indigenous  creators,  who,  in
dialogue  with  curators  and  academics  informed  by  decolonial  theories
and critical studies on race and ethnicity, have adopted a critical stance
against the hegemonic logic –  urban, white and male –  of the artistic field,
making visible the colonial roots of modern aesthetic valuation criteria
and promoting new ways of understanding and positioning Indigenous
art in the global scenario. In Peru, this dynamic has been particularly evi-
dent  in  the  case  of  artists  from  the  Amazon,  who,  unlike  their  Andean

SHRA’s  work  was  followed  by  two  important  exhibitions  that
highlighted  Amazonian  Indigenous  art:  El  ojo  verde.  Cosmovisiones
amazónicas  (The  Green  Eye.  Amazonian  Cosmovisions,  2000),  curated
by Pablo Macera and Gredna Landolt at the Fundación Telefónica; and
Serpiente  de  agua.  La  vida  indígena  en  la  Amazonía  (Water  Serpent.
Indigenous Life in the Amazon, 2003), curated by Landolt and Alexandre
Surrallés at the Estación Cultural Desamparados. Since then, Amazonian
Indigenous  art  has  emerged  as  one  of  the  most  dynamic  cultural
movements,  addressing  a  diversity  of  themes  that  include  Indigenous
worldviews,  the  rubber  era,  visionary  art  and  urban- popular  visuality.
While it is impossible to summarise in a few lines the development of this
scene over the last two decades –  and it would be arbitrary to choose spe-
cific milestones –  it is undeniable that the work driven mainly by artists,
academics  and  independent  curators  has  had  a  significant  impact.13  In
fact,  as  a  result  of  these  efforts,  a  growing  number  of  institutions  and
events focused on contemporary art have begun to recognise Amazonian
Indigenous art as an essential part of their narratives.

Locally, this dynamic became palpable when, in 2011, Murui and
Bora  artist  Brus  Rubio  won  second  place  in  the  XIV  Pasaporte  para  un
Artista  (Passport  for  an  Artist)  Competition  organised  by  the  French
Alliance of Lima and the French Embassy of Peru –  perhaps the most im-
portant award aimed at emerging artists at the national level. Likewise,
in  2015,  the  Museo  de  Arte  de  Lima  (MALI)  created  its  Amazonian
collection, committing to expand the representation of the region in its
contemporary  collection.  Furthermore,  a  regional  milestone  occurred
in  2017,  when  the  Museu  de  Arte  de  São  Paulo  Assis  Chateaubriand
(MASP) organised the first of several symposiums that laid the concep-
tual  foundations  for  the  exhibition  Indigenous  Histories  (2023– 2024),

an alternative to Western thinking and a tool for imagining post- capitalist
futures.  It  is  no  coincidence,  then,  that  only  in  the  years  following  the

seemed distant: representation in commercial galleries, participation in
international  fairs  and  biennials,  and  the  incorporation  of  their  works
into  the  collections  of  the  world’s  leading  museums.15  It  is  precisely  in
this context, traversed by deep contradictions, that we believe it is vital
to review the SHRA’s work and the ways in which its seminal labour has
shaped what we know today as Amazonian Indigenous art in Peru, laying
the foundations for its consolidation in the international contemporary
cultural panorama.

II

The  Seminario  de  Historia  Rural  Andina  (SHRA)  is  an  institute  of  the
Universidad Nacional Mayor de San Marcos (UNMSM), founded by his-
torian Pablo Macera in 1966 and dedicated to researching rural societies
in Peru. In the 1990s, the seminar began working with Indigenous artists
from the Andes and the Amazon to recover the memory of their peoples
using  the  painting- writing  equation  as  a  methodology  for  approaching
oral societies. Unlike other research projects that took the knowledge of
Indigenous and rural groups to inform their studies, the SHRA identified
early on that these stories should be told by the members of the commu-
nities themselves. Thus, they established collaborative projects between
artists and scholars. This way of working not only empowered the artists,
but also led to the development of works that would significantly define
the aesthetics of Amazonian Indigenous art in Peru.

During its early decades, the SHRA –  located within the facilities of
the university’s ‘Colegio Real’ –  focused its research on the economy and
agriculture of rural Andean societies to explain the socioeconomic and
cultural systems on which Peruvian society has been built throughout its
history.16 From its inception, the SHRA’s research was characterised by

sociology,  anthropology,  ethnohistory  and  literature.17  Collaborations

and linguists to collect, process and produce the oral and graphic memory

One  of  the  first  projects  was  carried  out  by  Macera  and  historian
Rosaura Andazábal with painter Carmelón Berrocal (Sarhua, Ayacucho,
1964– 1998).  Berrocal  migrated  to  Lima  in  1984  and  painted  first  for
the Asociación de Artistas Populares de Sarhua (Association of Popular
Artists  of  Sarhua,  ADAPS)  and  then  in  the  workshop  he  opened  with
his brother Pompeyo Berrocal. In 1994, Berrocal left the workshop and
began working at the SHRA where, in conversation with historians, he
wrote  and  illustrated  Sarhuino  stories  and  tales,  and  also  shared  his
knowledge about the territory, animals and plants of Sarhua.

Berrocal’s  creative  process  set  a  landmark  for  the  seminar’s  sub-
sequent  collaborations  with  other  artists.  In  addition  to  the  projects
undertaken with Berrocal, Macera and Andazábal continued their work
in  the  field  of  Andean  orality  and  aesthetics,  collaborating  with  Félix
Condori  Vilca  (Puno),  Leonidas  Zorayda  Jara  Palacios  (Ancash),  Jesús
Urbano Rojas (Ayacucho) and Genoveva Núñez Herrera (Cusco). It was
not until 1997 that the projects with Amazonian artists began. Macera
and historian Belén Soria worked with the Bora painter Víctor Churay,
the Shipibos Robert Rengifo, Lastenia Canayo, Elena Valera and Roldán
Pinedo,  and  the  Asháninkas  Enrique  and  Wilberto  Casanto.  As  in  the
case of Carmelón Berrocal, these artists represented through figurative
images and the written word the knowledge, daily experiences and ritual
practices of their respective communities. Based on these collaborations,
the  SHRA  organised  exhibitions  and  produced  various  publications,
reflecting on the transition from rural aesthetics to the urban context.

Prior  to  working  with  Indigenous  artists,  the  SHRA  had  already
conducted  research  on  the  Amazon  from  historical  and  archaeological
perspectives. However, as Soria points out, these investigations analysed
the region ‘from the viewpoint of the colonizing and mestizo society’.19
Although it is evident today that native communities are also producers of
history, many scholars of the time ‘were dominated by the erroneous con-
cept that societies lacking alphabetic writing were ahistorical’.20 A turning
point  for  the  SHRA  was  the  work  that  graphic  artist  Gredna  Landolt
developed with the teachers of the Programa de Formación de Maestros
Bilingües de la Amazonía Peruana (Bilingual Teacher Training Program

Landolt joined Formabiap in 1997 as a graphic consultant for the
development  of  educational  materials.  Very  early  on,  she  noticed  the
aesthetic richness of the images created by the Indigenous sages, who,
in  passing  on  the  knowledge  and  worldviews  of  their  communities,
captured  profoundly  personal  and  artistic  images.  Motivated  by  this
discovery, she proposed to Formabiap to publish a compilation of these
drawings, highlighting their aesthetic dimension. However, fearing that
their wisdom would be trivialised, Formabiap teachers denied permission
until 2000. In the meantime, Landolt shared with Macera her experience
in the programme. This exchange sparked the historian’s interest in ex-
ploring the potential of working with Amazonian community members.22
Therefore, inspired by the experience of Landolt at Formabiap, the SHRA
adopted  a  methodology  similar  to  the  one  already  employed  with  the
Sarhuino painter Carmelón Berrocal, but this time with a strong focus on
the Amazonian world.

On  the  other  hand,  after  obtaining  authorisation  from  the
Formabiap teachers in 2000, Landolt approached Fundación Telefónica
with  a  proposal  to  develop  a  publication  that  would  compile  the  work
produced through the programme. However, the foundation suggested
an  exhibition  accompanied  by  a  catalogue.  From  that  moment  on,
Landolt  began  to  collaborate  directly  with  each  Indigenous  teacher  or
sage, broadening and deepening the creation of a more extensive set of
drawings and paintings.23 In contrast to Formabiap’s original approach,
where the images functioned primarily as ethnographic explanations of
the Indigenous peoples’ way of life, knowledge and practices, Landolt’s
approach focused on highlighting the aesthetic and subjective value of
these representations.

The  result  of  this  process  was  presented  in  the  exhibition  El  ojo
verde:  Cosmovisiones  amazónicas,  co- curated  by  Landolt  and  Macera,
at  Fundación  Telefónica  in  2000.  It  is  worth  noting  that,  after  this  ex-
perience,  most  of  the  Formabiap  teachers  continued  their  path  in  the

III

The  SHRA’s  first  project  with  an  Amazonian  Indigenous  artist  was  the
one developed by Macera and Soria with the Bora painter Víctor Churay
(Pucaurquillo,  Loreto,  1972- Lima,  2002).  As  a  result  of  his  work  with
the  seminar,  Churay  represented  the  natural  environment,  the  myths,
stories,  and  ritual  practices  of  his  community  (see  Figure  8.1),  as  well
as the exploitation suffered by the Bora people during the rubber era. As
art historian María Eugenia Yllia states, it is within this framework that
Víctor Churay ‘began his vocation to investigate the history of his ethnic
group and strengthened his critical gaze in the face of the foreign elem-
ents that transformed his society’.24

In  1997,  the  Shipibo- Konibo  painter  Roger  Rengifo/ Chonomeni
(Iparia, Ucayali, 1967) also joined the SHRA. Using oil paint mixed with
tempera,  resins,  and  natural  dyes  on  canvas,  he  painted  the  scenarios
and magical characters of the Shipibo cosmovision. In 1998, Chonomeni
contacted the Shipibo- Konibo artist Elena Valera/ Bahuan Jisbe (Pucallpa,
Ucayali, 1968) to join the seminar.25 In this context, Valera began to paint
themes  of  the  daily  life,  worldview,  plants  and  animals  of  the  Shipibo-
Konibo world. She also taught painting to Roldán Pinedo/ Shoyan Sheca,
her partner at the time, with whom she produced many of her early works.
Asháninka  artist  Enrique  Casanto  (Puerto  Bermúdez,  Pasco,
1956)  joined  the  SHRA  in  early  1999.  During  this  time,  he  created
paintings  about  the  myths  and  stories  of  the  Ashaninka  spirits  of  the
forest,  as  well  as  legendary  warriors,  historical  characters  and  mytho-
logical figures linked to the origin of the Ashaninka clans and their his-
tory (see Figure 8.2). The Shipibo- Konibo artist Lastenia Canayo/ Pecon
Quena  (Roroboya,  Ucayali,  1962)  began  her  collaboration  with  the
SHRA in 2000 through the project Mothers/ Girls, dedicated to the com-
pilation  of  tales  and  short  stories  shared  by  native  mothers  with  their
daughters.26 Through different techniques such as painting, embroidery
and sculpture, Canayo began to figuratively represent the ibo or ‘owners’,
powerful beings who protect and regulate the use of plants, animals and
other elements of the forest. Canayo conveyed this knowledge, which is

The  work  with  the  artists  gave  rise  to  exhibitions  of  their  works,

de  Reserva  del  Perú  (Museum  of  the  Central  Reserve  Bank  of  Peru,

of Peruvian Culture), the gallery of Banco Continental, and the gallery
of Banco Wiesse, and later in the exhibition room of the ‘Colegio Real’
(inaugurated  in  2000),  where  the  SHRA  had  its  headquarters.  The
exhibitions always included a catalogue. The first publications, printed
in mimeograph, featured an introductory text by Macera, a text by the
curator of the exhibition (in the case of the Amazonian art exhibitions,
Belén Soria), and black and white reproductions of the exhibited works,
accompanied by relevant explanations. From the opening of the gallery
at  the  ‘Colegio  Real’,  each  exhibition  was  accompanied  by  a  colour
printed booklet with the curatorial text and photographs of the artworks
exhibited.

The  SHRA  developed  methodologies  that  aimed  to  change  the
hegemonic  Eurocentric  ways  in  which  academia  and  scientific  re-
search engaged with Indigenous and rural populations: describing and
legitimising their knowledge only after it had passed through the filter of
the researcher. In doing so, the work of the seminar was developed from
what we would today consider a decolonial practice with an important
focus on the self- determination and agency of the artists. One of the fun-
damental points was the collaborative work with the creators, in order
to give preeminence to their voices and perspectives. Soria says: ‘We the
researchers were simply the vehicles that allowed the empowerment of
these new artists.’27

In addition, the individual authorship of storytellers and painters
was  recognised.  Unlike  the  widespread  practice  of  considering
Indigenous artists only as representatives of a larger collectivity without
identifying  the  individual  features  of  their  production,  the  SHRA
encouraged them to sign their works and, even when Macera and other
members  of  the  seminar  bought  them,  they  still  retained  full  rights  to
their pieces. Moreover, the painters and narrators appeared as authors
of the catalogues that were developed around their artworks, while the
names of the academics were only mentioned within the interior of the
publications. Another important aspect of the SHRA is that it provided
the artists with a space to create, the logistical support to develop their
practice,  and  payment  for  their  work,  which  allowed  them  to  dedicate
several hours of their day to this labour. Belén Soria emphasises how this

At the SHRA, artists discussed with scholars the themes they could
represent in their paintings. Elena Valera mentions: ‘They always left me
free to do. So, they never told me to do this, no. But you can do, for ex-
ample, wild fauna, some wild birds, and also some worldviews. And that
was it. It was up to me how I did it. So, that was my creativity. But some-
times I was asked, I need this theme, and I also did that. I need, for ex-
ample, the feast of San Juan; or I need the great feast of the Shipibos, the

did  some  research  to  put  everything,  that  is,  to  express  what  I  under-

ask my grandmother and my aunts. And that’s how I worked.’29

The  seminar  encouraged  the  artists  to  both  recover  the  ances-
tral  knowledge  of  their  communities  and  investigate  the  materials  and
techniques  of  their  regions,  with  emphasis  on  the  rescue  of  traditional
practices  and  the  use  of  natural  elements.  Valera,  for  example,  recalls
that Macera and Soria encouraged her to work with the Shipibo- Konibo
technique of dyeing fabrics with mahogany to then paint on them with
other natural pigments. They even developed a project for the artist to
travel to Pucallpa to collect plants, seeds, tree barks, coloured earths and
other  natural  elements  to  make  the  new  dyes  for  the  paintings.  Elena
comments: ‘I went on a month- long trip far away, along the Pisqui River.
And then, when I came back, I did it, I worked on it.’30

The SHRA not only prioritised intellectual and artistic collaboration
with  painters  and  storytellers  but  also  placed  significant  emphasis  on
their personal well- being and that of their families. Within the seminar,
no  distinctions  were  made  between  academic  researchers  and  artists.
Shared lunches, birthday celebrations, and collective efforts to support
members facing economic or health emergencies fostered a sense of com-
munity and mutual care.

practices of ‘the repertoire’ can maintain and transmit the same meaning,

experiences  of  the  executants  and  the  new  contexts  in  which  they  are

stored and transmitted through ‘the repertoire’. Canadian Indigenous cur-

passed down orally over the course of countless generations continually
change … yet remain the same. These stories are always individualised
and communal, original and replicated, authored and unauthored’.32

In  many  cases,  however,  the  archive  and  ‘the  repertoire’  work  to-
gether  in  the  transmission  of  knowledge.  Many  practices  in  societies
with or without writing require both materiality and action at the same
time.33  Under  this  premise,  while  the  SHRA  projects  sought  to  transfer
changing  practices  to  the  stability  of  a  Western- style  archive,34  they
also recognised that ‘the memory of native peoples is activated by sym-
bolic representations: facial and body paintings, designs on pottery and
hunting marks, among other semiotic images’ that cannot be translated
exclusively  through  writing.35  Therefore,  they  decided  to  translate  the
oral simultaneously into written texts and images. First, they invited the
narrators to write their stories in a bilingual format (in their native lan-
guage and in Spanish) and then asked them to make drawings to comple-
ment the texts. ‘We wanted the texts produced in the SHRA to associate
painting and words as reciprocally complementary and suitable elements
to express the worldviews of the Amazonian peoples.’36 These processes
were carried out in different ways. ‘Some storytellers wanted to write and
illustrate their stories themselves. In this case, the drawing was an exten-
sion  of  the  word  that  materialised  their  experiences.  Others,  however,
preferred  to  tell  us  their  stories  through  drawings  rather  than  writing
them down, as was the case with Roldán Pinedo and Elena Valera. There
were also those who wrote and let other native painters illustrate their
stories. This happened with the Shipibo Herminio Vásquez (storyteller)
and Robert Rengifo (painter).’37

On the visual level, Amazonian artists in the SHRA predominantly
expressed  themselves  through  drawing  and  painting  under  figurative
modalities.  In  the  Amazonian  communities,  painting  has  traditionally

in the SHRA. Víctor Churay, for example, was already painting customs
and myths in his native Pucaurquillo for tourists who came to the com-

designs that contain Shipibo- Konibo wisdom), learned to draw and paint
in the Western way in a vocational training course at school.39

While the artists already had experience with figurative painting,
it is interesting that they chose this method for their work in recovering
the  oral  and  graphic  memory  of  their  respective  cosmogonic  environ-
ments.  The  work  at  SHRA  was  based  on  ‘establishing  meeting  points
between the written culture and the symbolic representations of native
cultures’.40  This  process  involved  the  recodification  of  orality  into  texts
and images, translating, as Yllia points out, ‘from a subaltern language to
a hegemonic one, from an Amazonian cultural context to a Limeño one’.41
What occurred, then, was what postcolonial and decolonial studies have
identified as contact zones: spaces of encounter between subordinated or
marginalised groups and dominant or ruling cultures.42 In these spaces of
conflict, acts of translation that enable dissent and negotiation can take
place.  Subordinated  or  marginalised  groups  take  and  select  from  the
materials transmitted by a dominant or metropolitan culture and reshape
them, adapting them to convey their own perspectives and interests. As
Pratt notes, ‘while subjugated peoples cannot readily control what em-
anates from the dominant culture, they do determine to varying extents
what they absorb into their own, and what they use it for’.43

Indeed, Soria mentions that ‘native storytellers perceived the value
of  literacy  to  convey  not  only  their  worldviews,  but  also  the  claims  of
their peoples who needed to use writing to “convey their messages to a
larger collective” and to spark debates about the problems afflicting the
Amazon’.44  Amazonian  creators  adopted  Western  conventions  such  as
the written word and figurative painting to express their knowledge and
communicate their concerns in a style that better suited the new content
developed in the SHRA and was legible to urban audiences. They thus
adopted  a  discourse  validated  from  dominant  perspectives.  The  use  of
Western conventions, however, not only helped them to be understood
by  urban  audiences,  but  ultimately  contributed  to  maintaining  their
ethnic identity in other contexts.

We believe that the method followed by the SHRA –  based on the
passage  from  the  oral  and  the  performative  to  writing  and  painting  –
shaped the way in which the idea of contemporary Indigenous art was
configured in Peru. At the heart of the projects carried out with the artists
was the intention of learning and preserving the knowledge of their com-
munities. It was, therefore, a method aligned more closely to anthropo-
logical or ethnographic frameworks, though it consistently acknowledged
the individuality of each production. In other words, each artist shared
the knowledge of their community through their unique experiences and
perspectives, expressing it in highly personal styles and forms.

V

Without a doubt, Amazonian Indigenous art is one of the most important
phenomena  of  contemporary  Peruvian  art  in  the  twenty- first  century.
Unlike the Indigenist artists of the early twentieth century, who, trained
in European fine arts, refrained from working with the languages of ver-
nacular  arts,  the  Indigenous  artists  of  the  Amazon  have  appropriated
the  Western  mediums  and  techniques  to  express  cultures  and  worlds
that are their own and that represent an alterity to the modern civilising
project.  The  work  of  the  SHRA  has  been  seminal  and  essential  in  dis-
mantling  what,  for  much  of  Peru’s  republican  history,  constituted  a
system  of  institutionalised  segregation  against  the  artistic  expressions
of Indigenous peoples. However, and perhaps unintentionally, this pro-
ject defined the ‘integration’ of Amazonian Indigenous art under a logic
closer to the modern than to the contemporary.

Indeed,  the  seminar’s  ethnographic  intent  and  working  method-
ology, expanded through its artists and circles of influence, led Peruvian
Amazonian  Indigenous  art  to  adopt,  primarily,  the  form  of  figurative
painting.45 Established as the hegemonic medium of Eurocentric artistic
modernity  in  the  nineteenth  and  twentieth  centuries,  and  apparently
superseded  by  contemporary  standards,  painting  then  became  the  cri-
terion of legitimisation for these previously marginalised artists. This, we
believe, may have limited the experimentation of Amazonian Indigenous
artists  with  other  media  such  as  performance,  video  or  installation  –
languages  that,  according  to  artists  from  other  latitudes,  allow  an  ex-
pression more aligned with the artistic practices of their communities.46
On the other hand, long standing traditional Amazonian techniques such
as  ceramics,  textiles  or  beads  –   which  preserve  the  geometric  designs

have  preserved  their  traditional  designs,  it  is  no  coincidence  that  their
works are frequently exhibited in frames evoking the Western pictorial
tradition, which we believe facilitates their reception by the urban public
and their insertion in the local and international market.47

The  entry  of  Indigenous  art  into  the  contemporary  art  circuit  is
not  free  of  contradictions  and  controversies.  A  case  worth  analysing  is
the latest edition of the Venice Biennale, Stranieri Ovunque (Foreigners
Everywhere),  curated  by  Brazilian  Adriano  Pedrosa,  director  of  the
Museu  de  Arte  de  São  Paulo  Assis  Chateaubriand  (MASP).  This  exhib-
ition  generated  great  expectations  for  its  potential  to  consolidate  the
presence of Latin American Indigenous art in the global circuits of con-
temporary  art,  positioning  itself  as  a  continuity  of  the  decolonial  pro-
gramming promoted by the MASP.48 However, although the number of
participating Indigenous artists was significantly higher than in previous
editions,  their  presentation  was  mostly  mediated  by  works  adjusted  to
pictorial  Western  canons,  which  limited  the  possibility  of  highlighting
other  aesthetic  genealogies.  Indeed,  art  historian  Natalia  Majluf  states
that in the biennale there was almost no ‘space for non- Western aesthetic
traditions or forms forged in the hybridity of the provinces farthest from
cosmopolitan  circuits’.49  According  to  the  author,  this  configured  ‘an

These  reflections  raise  a  central  question:  does  the  integration  of

co- optation, or can this art transform the dynamics of the contemporary
system by critically dialoguing with its languages, institutions and modes
of  legitimisation?  We  believe  that  answering  this  question  requires
listening  to  the  experiences  of  Indigenous  artists  who  confront,  in  first
person, the nuances of this system. For example, the Uitoto artist Rember
Yahuarcani/ Ribetuau describes how his entry into the contemporary art
world led him to reconfigure his pictorial practice in search of a personal
language  that  would  avoid  the  exoticisation  and  folklorisation  of  his
work, while vindicating its epistemological difference and its political di-
mension: ‘What motivated me to change my pictorial register was that
I did not want my work to be an ethnographic piece, not to be an object
of anthropological study but an object of art. Nor did I want to continue
explaining my work as Indigenous art, that this scene was a dance, that
another scene was Uitotos hunting or mambeando, and so on. I simply
wanted to hang my work and, like any other contemporary artist, I wanted
it to have the necessary elements and characteristics to speak for itself.’51

On the other hand, Jota Mombaça, a Brazilian artist and multidis-
ciplinary thinker, demonstrates how the growing presence of Black, mi-
grant, and Indigenous artists puts a strain on an elitist art system that,
paradoxically, is sustained and updated by the critical and speculative po-
tential of the aesthetics produced by non- hegemonic bodies. Identifying
herself as a ‘Black queer’, Mombaça stresses that access to the contem-
porary  art  world  for  minorities  is  always  mediated  and  conditioned  by
the  rhetoric  of  ‘white  alliances’,  which  has  maintained  a  system  of  re-
source  distribution  that  allows  those  in  positions  of  social  privilege  to
control the terms and agendas of the decolonial debate. In this context,
according to Mombaça, the condition for racialised artists to enter these
spaces is to operate ‘almost exclusively as a badge of their position and
not as a person’.52

As  Mombaça  rightly  points  out,  questioning  and  dismantling  the
power  dynamics  inscribed  in  the  art  world  requires  not  only  ‘opening
spaces’  or  ‘giving  voice’,  but  redistributing  positions  of  power  and
resources more equitably. Precisely for this reason, we believe that the
work of the SHRA is a paradigmatic case in Peru. Despite being an insti-
tution rooted in the heart of academia, its interdisciplinary team always
had the conviction that self- determination was essential when working
with  artists  from  Indigenous  communities.  To  this  end,  the  SHRA  not
only broke with traditional academic hierarchies, prioritising their voices
and  perspectives,  but  also  underwent  a  process  of  critical  deconstruc-
tion  of  its  own  disciplinary  frameworks  –   historical,  ethnographic,  an-
thropological, among others –  which allowed for the full recognition of
the individual artistic authorship of the Indigenous artists with whom it
collaborated.  This  shift  in  focus  was  crucial  in  transforming  the  SHRA
into  a  flexible  and  malleable  ‘contact  zone’  where  Indigenous  artists
could  interact  with  hegemonic  structures  on  their  own  terms,  challen-
ging the colonial logics of erasure that had marked the history of local
art.  Moreover,  as  we  have  pointed  out,  the  seminar  paved  the  way  for
a pioneering generation of Amazonian Indigenous artists to claim their
central place in contemporary Peruvian art, while also becoming a plat-
form for the resignification of the language of art in Peru and for dialogue
within the global art circuit.
